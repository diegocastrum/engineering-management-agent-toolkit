# Career Vision Exercise

Source: James Stanier, *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, chapter 18 ("The Crystal Ball").

Most career conversations don't get past the next promotion. Stanier's argument: spend a few hours, once a year, working through a deeper exercise that produces a long-term vision and a concrete short-term plan. The exercise has structure — six steps over multiple sessions when delivered to a report.

## When this skill is loaded

Identify mode:

- **Self-exercise** — user wants to do this for themselves
- **Delivery prep** — user wants to deliver this to a direct report through coaching sessions
- **Stage check** — user is in the middle of the exercise and needs help with a specific step

## When to do this exercise

- Once a year, after performance reviews (so growth feedback feeds in)
- After a major life change for the report (new role, new manager, life event)
- When a report says they "don't know where they want to go" — that's the signal

Not for every weekly 1-1. This is a deeper exercise, done deliberately.

## The six steps (overview)

When delivering to a report, this becomes a sequence of coaching sessions:

1. **Past timeline exercise** — looking back over 10 years
2. **Ten-years coaching session** — using the past to dream the future
3. **Reviewing their vision** — refining the vision statement
4. **Input, scoring, homework** — peer feedback on competencies + draft plan
5. **One-year coaching session** — building skills backlog
6. **Ongoing review** — quarterly check-ins on the backlog

For self-use, the user does each step alone (no coaching session needed; they're coaching themselves).

## Step 1: Look back ten years

Draw a horizontal line representing the last 10 years of life. Plot significant events on it — career *and* personal:

- Jobs, roles, promotions
- Education, training
- Geographic moves
- Major projects
- Personal milestones (relationships, family, losses)

The point: most people radically underestimate how much can happen in 10 years. Looking at the actual filled-out timeline expands the horizon for the future.

After plotting, reflect on what played a role in each event. Categories:

- **Training** — formal education, courses, self-directed learning
- **Luck** — being in the right place; meeting the right person
- **People** — mentors, advocates, peers who shaped trajectory
- **Forethought** — decisions made deliberately with the future in mind
- **Hindsight** — lessons learned that you wish you'd applied earlier

This pass tunes you to recognize that the next 10 years will involve all five — and you can influence training, forethought, and (to some extent) people deliberately.

## Step 2: Coach toward a 10-year vision

This is the heart of the exercise. The trap: people aim too low — they describe the next 2 years and call it a vision.

If running this with a report, your job is to **coach, not direct**. Keep the thought bubble over their head. Ask provocative questions to push past the safe answers.

Prompts that move people to think bigger:

- How do you feel about your current job? What do you enjoy *most*? What do you wish you could change?
- How do your skills map to the most innovative work in industry right now? Is that where you want to be heading?
- Are there mornings you can't wait to get to work? Why?
- Are there mornings you can't bear lifting the covers? Why?
- *Imagine yourself waking up 10 years from now.* What does the bedroom look like? Where do you live? What do you see out the window? You make breakfast — who's there? You get dressed and look in the mirror — what are you wearing? Where are you going, and how? Embody that future person.

Listen for understatement. "I want to work in product" might actually mean "I want to be the CPO at my own successful startup." Probe.

## Step 3: Write the vision statement

After the coaching session, the person writes their vision as a structured document. Stanier's format:

```markdown
# My Vision

## 10-year vision
[Compelling first-person statements. "I will be [role] at [type of company]
in [type of place]. We will [mission]. I will be living in [location] with
[family/situation]."]

## Most rewarding achievements in the last 12 months
[What's been most rewarding and how does it support the future vision?]

## What excites me most about my current role
[How does that excitement project forward?]

## Do I see myself managing people in the future?
[If yes — what kind? What do you like about it?]

## Formal qualifications and their role
[Do current qualifications matter? Do I need more?]
```

The "I will" language matters. Make the vision feel touchable.

### Getting more input

After writing, get external pressure-test:

- Send to partner, close friends, trusted family. What do they think? Realistic? Overambitious? Underambitious?
- Find five people whose current state matches your 10-year vision. Look at their profiles. How did they get there? What do they talk about? Who do they interact with?

## Step 4: Review the vision

After they've drafted, schedule another session.

- Read it together. Does it resonate now that time has passed?
- Is it actually a *vision* (compelling, magnetic) or a list of tasks?
- What needs sharpening, what feels generic?
- Is it underambitious — does it still just describe the next promotion?

Probe for what's missing. Refine.

## Step 5: From vision to skills backlog

Now move from 10 years to the next year. Backward-chain:

- What needs to be true in 5 years to reach the 10-year vision?
- What needs to be true in 3 years?
- What needs to be true in 2 years?
- What needs to be true in 1 year?
- What do I need to do in the next 6 months?

The 1-year answer becomes the **plan statement**. Concrete and measurable.

```markdown
# My Plan

This year, I need to [primary focus statement].

I know I struggle with [X]. I need to practice this by [specific action].

I need to become confident at [Y]. I can do this by [specific behavior].

I don't know enough people in [Z]. I need to [specific outreach].

I'll know I've succeeded this year if [observable outcome].
```

### Score current competencies

Using the career progression framework (see motivation-and-fit skill or your company's career ladder), rate yourself on each competency at your current level. Get peer feedback on the same competencies from 3-5 colleagues to triangulate.

Where there's a gap between your self-rating and others' ratings — that's a discussion topic. Sometimes you're harder on yourself; sometimes others see something you don't.

### Build the skills backlog

A Trello-style board or simple list. Each card = a skill or competency to practice with concrete actions.

| Backlog | Doing | Done |
|---|---|---|
| Run 10 1-1s using contracting | Lead my first hiring interview | Wrote first ADR |
| Give a department talk | Learning to write structured weekly summary | Audited my calendar |
| Mentor a junior on Go | ... | ... |

Each card has comments where you record progress. Review the board regularly.

## Step 6: Ongoing review

- **Quarterly:** check the backlog with the report. What's moved? What hasn't? Why?
- **Every 6 months:** revisit the plan statement. Still right? Adjust.
- **Annually:** redo the full exercise. The vision evolves; that's normal.

## Output format

When the user is doing the exercise themselves, produce documents in the formats above. When delivering to a report, produce session prep + the documents they'll fill out as homework.

For session prep:

```markdown
# Session [N]: [topic]

## Goal
[What this session achieves]

## Coaching questions to use
- ...

## Likely sticking points and how to handle
- ...

## Homework after this session
- ...
```

## Forcing functions

- **If the vision reads like a job description for "next role"**, push to expand the horizon. 10 years is the unit.
- **If the user (or report) skips the past-timeline exercise**, encourage doing it. The expansion of what's possible comes from seeing how much actually happened in 10 years.
- **If the plan statement has no measurable outcome**, refine. "Become a better manager" isn't measurable; "lead 4 successful hires and run 10 effective 1-1s on contracting" is.
- **If the user is delivering this to a report and starts directing rather than coaching**, redirect. The vision has to come from the report, not from the manager.
- **If they've never built a skills backlog before**, set up a simple Trello board or doc with a small first batch. Don't over-engineer.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't generate a generic vision statement for the user. The exercise depends on the work *they* do; predigested visions don't stick.
- Don't push reports to align their vision with the company's needs. Sometimes the right vision means leaving — that's fine and useful to know.
- Don't compress the exercise into a single meeting. It's a sequence over time. Compressing it kills the work that happens between sessions.
- Don't conflate this with performance reviews. Different cadence, different purpose.
