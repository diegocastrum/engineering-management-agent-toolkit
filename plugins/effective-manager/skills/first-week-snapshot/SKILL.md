# First Week Snapshot

Source: James Stanier, *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, chapter 1 ("A New Adventure").

A new manager's first week feels like impostor syndrome made manifest. The cure is structure. Stanier's argument: don't try to act managerial — *gather information* by triangulating three views, then act on what the data shows.

## When this skill is loaded

Figure out where the user is:

- **Day 1-3** — meeting people, getting oriented. Help with intro meeting structure and ice-breaker questions.
- **Day 4-5** — synthesizing. Walk them through the snapshot Venn diagram.
- **End of week 1** — building action list. Help them sort observations into buckets.
- **Week 2+** — they've waited too long but the framework still applies. Get the snapshot done now.

## The snapshot: three overlapping views

The core exercise. Three sources of observations, overlaid like a Venn diagram:

1. **Your own observations** — what you see directly as you work with the team. Limited in week 1 because you've just arrived.
2. **Your manager's observations** — how they see the team's performance from outside.
3. **Your team's observations** — how the team sees itself, gathered through 1-1s with each member.

The four intersecting regions reveal where to act:

| Region | What it means | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Alignment** (all three agree) | Everyone observes the same thing — good or bad | Confirms reality; can move forward confidently |
| **Poor downward communication** (you + manager, not team) | The team is unaware of an issue leadership sees | Surface it carefully in 1-1s or team meeting |
| **Poor upward communication** (you + team, not manager) | The team is dealing with something leadership doesn't see | Raise it with your manager |
| **False beliefs** (manager + team, but not you) | Both have an assumption you can see is wrong | Challenge with your outside perspective |

The regions apply to positive issues too — a team performing well that doesn't realize it needs addressing.

## Initial 1-1s with each team member

In your first week, schedule 30 minutes with each direct report. Don't make these formal — coffee or a private room. The two goals:

1. Get them to describe **what the team is responsible for** and **what they're currently working on**.
2. Invite their opinion on **what's going well** and **what's most challenging or needs improving**.

Then break the ice with open-ended questions (use whichever land naturally):

- How's your week going?
- What are you working on at the moment?
- What was the last thing the team shipped that you were really proud of?
- What would you say the team is primarily responsible for?
- How are your teammates doing?
- What's your favorite thing about working here?
- What about your least favorite?
- What's the one thing nobody wants to speak up about?
- What's the one thing you think I could help you with?

**Listen, don't talk.** This isn't the meeting to share your vision. Note key themes. Thank them for their time. Confirm any actions back to them.

## Initial meeting with your manager

Be proactive — book it yourself. Use the same open-ended questioning approach but with a more guided agenda:

- Their opinion of the team and its performance
- Details of individuals: superstars or struggling?
- Framing of current project: how long, how much has it changed?
- Their thoughts on how best to work with them
- General advice about how to do your role in the organization

End by scheduling a weekly recurring 1-1 with them.

## Inheriting a team vs. starting fresh

If the user has been **promoted from within** or is **inheriting a team after their old manager left**, two extra dynamics:

- **Relationships are changing.** Close peers may now be subordinates. Acknowledge it directly: "This is going to be weird and I'll make mistakes — tell me when I do."
- **Watch for the candidate who didn't get the job.** If someone else applied for your role and didn't get it, name it in your 1-1 with them and talk it through honestly.
- **Don't immediately change everything.** The team has muscle memory and existing processes. Spend the first month learning *why* things work the way they do before reorganizing them.

For a **brand new team in a brand new company**, you have less context but more freedom. Lean harder on the team's observations because you have almost no own observations yet.

## What to set up in week 1

While you're gathering observations, get the infrastructure in place:

- **Weekly recurring 1-1s** with each direct report — same time, same place each week, private room, 1 hour to start. Ask each person their preferred time of day; mid-morning Tuesday–Thursday is most reliable.
- **Weekly recurring 1-1 with your manager** — same setup.
- **A shared notes doc per direct report** — for rolling agenda items.
- **A notebook (or phone) for capturing observations** as you go.

## Building the action list

By end of week 1, sort observations into buckets:

- **Items to discuss with your team** — false beliefs you observed, poor downward communication issues
- **Items to discuss with your manager** — poor upward communication, anything they should know
- **Items to investigate further** — things you don't have enough context on yet
- **Items to action yourself** — quick fixes you can just do

Prioritize ruthlessly. A first month with 30 issues to tackle is a setup for failure. Pick the 3-5 highest-impact things and start there.

## Output format

When helping with prep mode, produce a list of ice-breaker questions tailored to what the user knows about that person.

When helping with synthesis, produce the snapshot as a structured document:

```markdown
# Team Snapshot — [Team Name]

## Alignment (all three views agree)
- ...

## Poor downward communication (you + manager only)
- ...

## Poor upward communication (you + team only)
- ...

## False beliefs (manager + team, contradicts your view)
- ...

## Action list
### Discuss with team
- ...
### Discuss with manager
- ...
### Investigate further
- ...
### Action myself
- ...
```

## Forcing functions

- **If the user wants to "make changes" in week 1**, push back. You don't have enough information yet. Gather first.
- **If the user hasn't done individual 1-1s with each report**, that's the first action — even if uncomfortable.
- **If the user is heavy on observations from one source** (e.g., only their manager's view), flag it. The snapshot needs all three.
- **If the user says "the team is fine, nothing to fix"** after a single week, ask what they're missing. Either they're not looking hard enough or they got lucky.
- **If the user is trying to impress** instead of learn, redirect. Week 1 is about humility and curiosity, not authority.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't generate generic "first 90 day plan" templates. The snapshot is the plan.
- Don't recommend big team-wide announcements in week 1.
- Don't treat the snapshot as something to publish to the team. It's the user's working document.
