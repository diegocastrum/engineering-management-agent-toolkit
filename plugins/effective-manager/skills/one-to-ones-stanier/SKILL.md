# One-to-Ones (Stanier-style)

Source: James Stanier, *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, chapter 4 ("One-to-Ones").

1-1s are the highest-leverage activity a manager has. Stanier's argument: they aren't status meetings, they aren't optional, and you need a *contracting exercise* to set them up well from day one.

## When this skill is loaded

Identify mode:

- **First 1-1 with a new report** — use contracting exercise
- **Prep mode** — agenda for upcoming 1-1
- **Reflect mode** — notes from a 1-1 + extracting actions
- **Diagnostic mode** — 1-1s have drifted, feel wrong, or aren't working
- **Setup mode** — user has never run 1-1s before and needs the basics

## Setup mode: the fundamentals

If the user is just starting, lock these in:

- **Cadence**: weekly, every week, no exceptions. Steady rhythm matters more than perfect timing.
- **Duration**: 1 hour to start.
- **Location**: private — meeting room or quiet corner, not breakout area or near desks.
- **Same time, same place** each week. Don't move them unless absolutely necessary.
- **Shared notes doc per report** — Google Docs or similar. Rolling agenda lives here. Both sides add to it during the week.

If remote: video call with both cameras on. Not phone, not chat.

## First 1-1: contracting

A contracting session is the first 1-1 with any new direct report. It's structured. The point is to make the relationship's terms explicit instead of assumed.

Send the questions ahead so they can think. Work through them together in the meeting and capture answers in the shared doc.

**The five contracting questions:**

1. **Which areas would you like the most support with?** Don't suggest answers if they struggle — let it sit. Solutions aren't required yet; you'll revisit.

2. **How would you like to receive feedback and support?** Probe with examples. "If I disagreed with your technical direction on a whiteboard with the team watching, would you want me to say so in front of everyone, or pull you aside afterward, or send it in writing later?"

3. **What could be a challenge of us working together?** Surfaces initial impressions, gaps in skill alignment, awkward relationship dynamics. If they recently applied for the role you got, this is where you address it directly.

4. **How might we know if the support I'm offering isn't going well?** Explore each other's signs of frustration, anger, disappointment. Some people get loud; some go quiet and stew.

5. **How confidential is the content of our meetings?** What can you act on directly? What requires their permission? What can you relay to your own manager by default?

The contracting exercise can be revisited periodically — every 6-12 months or when something changes.

## Prep mode: what to put in the agenda

The shared doc *is* the agenda. Both sides drop items during the week as things come up. Pre-meeting, scan it and add anything missing. **Set a recurring to-do reminder** to prep the agenda day-of.

Good agenda items:
- Observations from the past week — work, team dynamics, anything to praise or probe
- A deep dive into a project, architecture decision, or piece of code they own
- Updates from elsewhere they'd find interesting — your skip 1-1, leadership news, hiring
- Coaching topics — something they're stuck on, or could grow into

If the agenda is thin, lean on coaching mode or pull from the topic ideas section below.

## Reflect mode: notes and actions

During the 1-1, take notes in the shared doc — yes, visibly, while you talk. Some teams cast it to a TV. Use bullets, bold for actions.

After the 1-1, the recurring to-do task should have two subtasks:

1. **Prepare notes ahead of meeting**
2. **Put actions in to-do list**

Always move actions assigned to *you* into your to-do list. Nothing destroys trust faster than a manager who doesn't follow up on what was raised. Most people have experienced this and assume it of all managers.

## How to actually run the meeting

**It's their meeting, not yours.** Aim for them to do 70% of the talking. Use leading questions:

- "How has your work been going this week?"
- "What are you working on today?"
- "Tell me about those production issues last week."
- "How are you feeling about the June deadline?"
- "How best could we ensure we've got the right metrics being logged ahead of time?"

**Silence is golden.** Don't fill every pause. The best parts of conversations often emerge after a trailing-off silence. "...and that's the problem, I guess. I just have no idea why they aren't helping us more." Let it land before responding.

**Don't make it a status meeting.** Status lives elsewhere (tickets, standup, chat). Touch on updates if needed, then move on. Probe deeper: "How could we deploy that into production quicker?" "Is this the right technical approach?" "Have you seen open source that could solve this?" — you don't need to know the answer.

## Topic ideas when the agenda is thin

- **Architecture deep dive** — ask them to walk you through part of the system. You learn; they surface weak spots.
- **Process deep dive** — how many steps to ship? Could any be removed?
- **A relevant article** — something from Hacker News, an open source project. Always loops back to the work.
- **Teaching** — share something you're learning (delegation, hiring, etc.)
- **Department or company direction** — what do they think? Reservations?
- **Collecting feedback** — anyone been particularly helpful recently? Pass it on.
- **Sharing something you're working on** — a JD draft, your own coding side project, a roadmap discussion with the PM. They like seeing what their manager does.

The connecting themes: sharing, building trust, working through technical and interpersonal problems together.

## Diagnostic mode: when 1-1s feel wrong

Match the complaint:

- **"It's just a status meeting"** — move status to async. Open with "What's on your mind?" instead. If they have nothing prepared, use a topic idea above.

- **"They have nothing to say"** — sometimes true; some senior people genuinely don't need weekly. Try bi-weekly with mutual agreement. But check: are they hiding things? Disengaged? About to quit? Ask: "How honestly are we able to talk in these? What would have to change for them to feel more useful?"

- **"It always tails off after 20 minutes"** — that's fine some weeks. Don't pad. End early when there's nothing to discuss. But if it's consistent, the relationship is too distant for an hour — fix the relationship.

- **"It feels like therapy"** — see "You are not a therapist" below.

- **"They keep cancelling / I keep cancelling"** — the cadence is broken. Re-anchor it. Mid-morning Tuesday-Thursday holds up best. If it's chronic, name it directly: this is the most important hour of our week together.

- **"Pulling teeth — they won't open up"** — you may be talking too much. Try the silence test. Try a coaching conversation instead of probing. Try going for a walk instead of sitting across a table.

## Pulling the Andon Cord

If either of you feels uncomfortable with a topic — too personal, too off-topic, needs someone else's help — either can call it. "Let's pause this; I'm not sure I'm the right person, can we bring HR in?" or "This is getting heavier than I expected, can we revisit next week?"

The metaphor: in Toyota production lines, any worker can pull the cord to halt the line and bring in support. Same principle in 1-1s.

## You are not a therapist

A 1-1 sometimes turns into a venting session or, rarely, surfaces real distress. Be clear about the line:

- You can listen empathetically. You should.
- You shouldn't try to diagnose, treat, or be the primary support for someone in mental health distress.
- If you sense someone is struggling beyond what a manager can help with, say so: "I sense you may not be feeling your best self. I want to make sure you get some external support if you need it." Suggest HR, EAP, a therapist.
- Never diagnose. Never assume. Wait until they tell you something is wrong before treating it as such.

This is sensitive territory. The goal is to be a supportive manager without crossing into a role you're not trained for.

## Output format

For prep mode, produce a short agenda:

```markdown
# 1-1 with [name] — [date]

## Standing items (their doc)
- ...

## Items I want to bring up
- ...

## Topic suggestion if agenda is thin
- ...

## Things to ask about
- ...
```

For reflect mode, structure the notes:

```markdown
# 1-1 with [name] — [date]

## Discussed
- ...

## Decisions
- ...

## Actions — me
- [ ] ...

## Actions — them
- [ ] ...

## Follow up next week
- ...
```

## Forcing functions

- **If user hasn't done contracting with a new report**, push for it as the first 1-1. It's worth the awkwardness.
- **If 1-1s are status updates**, name it. The fix is to move status to async and use the hour for everything else.
- **If user is doing all the talking**, flag the 70% rule.
- **If actions from previous 1-1s haven't moved**, that's the trust problem. Address it directly with the report and own it.
- **If user is delaying or moving 1-1s frequently**, push back. The whole system depends on the steady cadence.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't generate generic "rapport-building" prompts. Use the contracting questions or the topic ideas list.
- Don't suggest 30-minute 1-1s as a default. Start at 60 — you can always shorten later.
- Don't treat the 1-1 as a venue for performance reviews. Those happen separately, with prep, in dedicated sessions.
