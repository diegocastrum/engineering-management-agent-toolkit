# Career Growth

Source: Camille Fournier, *The Manager's Path*, chapter 4 ("Cultivating Careers"), chapter 9 ("Writing a Career Ladder"), and the threads on managing yourself throughout.

Two distinct things live here: **growing your team members' careers** (your job as a manager) and **growing your own** (your job as a person). The skill handles both.

## When this skill is loaded, identify whose career

- **Theirs (a report's)** — "X wants to be a tech lead, how do I help?" / "Is Y ready for promotion?"
- **Mine (the user's)** — "I want to grow into a director role" / "Should I take this management track?"
- **Structural** — designing or evolving the ladder itself, calibration across the team, equity in promotions

Ask if not clear.

## Growing a report's career

The conversation starts with this question — and the user should be able to answer it about every direct report:

> **What does this person want, and what do they need to learn to get there?**

If you don't know, your next 1-1 has a topic. Use prompts like:
- "Where do you want to be in 2 years? 5 years?"
- "What kinds of work do you find most energizing?"
- "Have you thought about whether you want to manage people eventually?"
- "What's a project that would feel like a real stretch right now?"

People often don't have crisp answers. That's fine — surface the rough shape and revisit.

### Identifying promotion readiness

For each report, periodically (every quarter or two) ask:

1. **What's their current level?**
2. **What's the next level?** What's actually different — skills, scope, impact, leadership?
3. **Are they currently exhibiting some of the next-level behaviors?** Many companies require "acting at the next level before being promoted to it" to avoid the Peter Principle.
4. **What's the gap?** Specific behaviors / experiences / impact they need to demonstrate.
5. **What's the path?** What project, what scope expansion, what mentorship would close the gap?

### Early-career promotions (E2 → E3, etc.)

For people in their first 1–3 years out of school:
- Promotion is usually default, *unless something is wrong*. Companies often have "up or out" policies at these levels.
- Your job is to **not get in their way** more than it is to push them forward.
- Make sure they're learning to estimate their work, getting it done within the estimates, learning from mistakes, participating in on-call, engaging in team planning.

### Mid-career to senior (E3 → senior, senior → staff)

This is where promotions get harder and slower. There are fewer opportunities to demonstrate the kind of leadership / scope / impact required.

Your job:
- **Identify promotion-worthy projects** — work that would naturally produce the artifacts a promotion case needs (cross-team impact, technical leadership, project leadership).
- **Assign them** to people close to promotion, or encourage those people to volunteer.
- **Teach them how the game is played** at your company. Transparency about the promotion process is itself a fairness move. People who understand the criteria don't waste effort on the wrong things.
- **Stretch projects** are the lever. The right project at the right time is what produces the promotion case.

### Senior to staff and beyond

These promotions are no longer "up or out." A senior engineer can stay senior forever and that's fine. Same for senior managers.

For people who want to advance further:
- Senior promotions usually require **scope** that the current team may not provide
- Sometimes the answer is moving to a different team or a different problem space
- Tech leads, mentors, and architectural contributors at this level are demonstrating leadership through influence, not authority — the promotion case is harder to assemble

### When someone's stuck

The "Coaching Someone Out of the Company" pattern from chapter 4. Symptoms:
- Doing OK work but not progressing for a long period
- You've told them what the next level requires, multiple times
- They go back to their comfort zone every time
- They're losing energy / engagement

The kind move: be direct. "I've told you what the next level looks like, and I haven't seen the change. I don't think this team / company is where you'll grow next. I'd help you find a fit elsewhere."

This isn't firing. They're meeting expectations at their level. It's acknowledging that growth requires a different environment, and being honest about it rather than letting them stagnate.

(This crosses into hard-conversations territory.)

## Career mentorship beyond promotion

Promotion is one form of growth. Others matter too:
- **Skill depth** — getting truly good at something they're already doing
- **Skill breadth** — new areas (frontend → backend, individual → leading, IC → management)
- **Visibility** — speaking, writing, open source, conference talks
- **Network** — connections within and outside the company

For each report, periodically ask: which of these are they actively investing in? Is there one that would compound?

### The mentorship vs. management distinction

Fournier's point: not every senior person on your team wants to manage. Some want to stay deeply technical. Both paths are valid; your ladder should reflect that.

For senior ICs who don't want to manage but still want to grow:
- Mentorship of more junior engineers is part of the senior IC job, not optional
- Project leadership without people-management is the typical scope expansion
- Architectural / strategic impact starts to matter more than feature output

## The user's own career

When the user is thinking about their own growth:

### The IC vs. management decision

If the user is debating the path: Fournier dedicates a chapter-3 section to the **imagined vs. real life** of both tracks. Both have real costs and rewards. Common traps:

- **Choosing management to avoid being "left behind"** when peers move up — bad reason. Many companies have IC ladders that go as high.
- **Choosing management because someone told you you'd be good at it** — fine, but make sure *you* want it. The job is harder than it looks and emphatically different from what made you a strong engineer.
- **Choosing IC because management seems unpleasant** — also fine, but recognize that staying technical past a point makes it harder to move into management later, not easier.

The decision isn't permanent. Fournier explicitly notes that switching back to IC after a management stint is common and valid.

### Growing as a manager

For someone already managing:
- **What level are you at?** (Team lead → multiple teams → managing managers → senior leadership)
- **What's the next level?** What's actually different about it?
- **Are you doing any of those things now?** Most growth from one management level to the next comes from voluntarily taking on responsibilities you don't technically have.

Specific growth moves at each level:
- **Team lead → managing multiple teams**: develop tech leads who can own their teams; learn to delegate complex frequent tasks; spend less time in code, more on strategy.
- **Multiple teams → managing managers**: practice skip-levels; learn to hold managers accountable rather than doing their jobs for them; develop instincts for organizational debugging.
- **Managing managers → senior leadership**: strategy becomes the main job; build relationships with cross-functional peers; learn to "disagree and commit."

### Self-management

A recurring Fournier theme: the biggest growth lever at every level is managing yourself. Specifically:

- **Time management** — distinguishing important from urgent (most managers spend too much on urgent-but-unimportant)
- **Emotional regulation** — particularly around criticism, conflict, and ambiguity
- **Curiosity** — defaulting to "tell me more" rather than judging or attacking
- **Self-awareness** — knowing what triggers you, what drains you, what you're avoiding

These aren't "soft skills." They're the multipliers on every other skill.

### Finding mentors and coaches

The further up you go, the less likely your manager is to be a great coach. Build external sources:
- A coach (paid, even if your company won't pay)
- Peers at your level at other companies (a small group, low-volume)
- Mentors who've been where you're going
- Reading: Fournier recommends several specific books (Andy Grove's *High Output Management*, Patrick Lencioni's *Five Dysfunctions*, Brené Brown's *Daring Greatly*, Marshall Goldsmith's *What Got You Here Won't Get You There*, L. David Marquet's *Turn the Ship Around*)

## Designing or evolving a career ladder

If the user is creating or rewriting a ladder, walk through Fournier's chapter-9 lessons:

1. **Don't write it alone.** Get senior engineers and managers to draft the parts that affect them. Their buy-in is essential.
2. **Look at examples.** Many companies publish their ladders now. Use them as starting points, not as templates to copy wholesale — what works for one company often fails for another (Fournier's own first ladder was a flop because she copied a friend's without enough adaptation).
3. **Be detailed.** Vague ladders create gaming. Specific behavioral descriptions reduce it.
4. **Two documents:**
   - Short spreadsheet for side-by-side comparison across levels
   - Long-form descriptions that read like a performance review of someone at that level
5. **Salary bands:**
   - Narrow bands and many levels early-career — supports frequent promotions and raises
   - Wide bands and fewer levels at senior — accommodates strong performers who aren't ready for the next level
6. **Breakpoint level**: define where "up or out" stops being a rule. Usually around senior engineer. Below: people advance or leave. At and above: indefinite tenure is fine.
7. **Keystone promotions**: certain promotions (senior, staff, principal, director, VP) should be recognized publicly. People need something to aim at beyond the next pay bump.
8. **Split management and IC tracks** above senior. Don't force people into management to advance.
9. **Mentorship as a requirement** for promotion above mid-career — Fournier specifically recommends this. Even on the IC track, you can't get to senior+ without having demonstrated leadership through mentorship or tech-leading.
10. **Years of experience**: don't use as a hard gate, but use as a sanity check. Staff engineers need maturity that takes time to develop.
11. **Expect to evolve it.** The first version will be wrong. Plan to revise within a year.

## Forcing functions

When working through career questions:

- **If the user can't name what the next level requires** for a report — fix that first. You can't help someone get promoted to a level you can't define.
- **If the user has been telling a report "soon" for multiple cycles** without it happening — name the gap. Soon-without-specifics is unkind.
- **If the user is debating their own management transition** and waffling — ask what *they* want, not what their boss wants for them. Pressure into management without desire is a setup for misery.
- **If a high performer hasn't been promoted in a long time** — investigate. Either the level expectations aren't actually being met (and they're not really high-performing at the next level), or you're losing them.
- **If "everyone deserves a promotion" pressure** is mounting — push back. Promotions are signals of operating at a new level, not seniority rewards. Inflating them dilutes the meaning.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't pretend career paths are linear. They're not.
- Don't recommend ambition for ambition's sake. Some people thrive at their level and that's fine.
- Don't help the user write a vague "growth plan" with no concrete projects or behaviors. That's a checkbox, not a plan.
- Don't generate corporate-speak. Career conversations are between two humans.
