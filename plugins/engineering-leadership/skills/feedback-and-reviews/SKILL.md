# Feedback and Performance Reviews

Source: Camille Fournier, *The Manager's Path*, chapter 4 ("Creating a Culture of Continuous Feedback" and "Performance Reviews").

The core rule that organizes everything in this skill: **no surprises**. If feedback first appears in a performance review, you failed earlier. Reviews are a synthesis step, not a delivery step.

## When this skill is loaded, identify the mode

- **In-the-moment feedback** — something just happened (good or bad) and the user is deciding whether/how to say something.
- **Continuous feedback planning** — user wants to build the habit; nothing specific has happened yet.
- **Review writing** — user is drafting a performance review and needs to synthesize.
- **Pre-review preparation** — review season is coming and the user wants to make sure nothing in any review will be a surprise.

## In-the-moment feedback

If the user says "X did Y, should I say something?" — the answer is almost always yes, and almost always soon. The decision tree:

1. **Is this praise?** Give it now, briefly, and in public if the person is OK with public praise (check what they said in their new-hire intake). The single biggest under-used lever for new managers is more frequent specific praise.

2. **Is this corrective?** Three sub-questions:
   - **Is it a one-off or a pattern?** One-offs from someone with a good track record can be mentioned lightly or skipped. Patterns must be named.
   - **Did it affect the team or just irritate you?** If it's the team, address it. If it's just you, sit with it for 24 hours — sometimes it's your reaction, not their behavior.
   - **Can you tie it to a concrete example?** If yes, deliver soon (within a day or two), in private, calmly. If no, wait until you have one. Vague feedback is unfair.

3. **Is this behavior public and harmful?** (e.g., someone is being rude to a colleague in a meeting.) This is the rare case where Fournier upends "praise in public, criticize in private" — address it in the moment, neutrally and briefly: "Please don't speak to people that way; it's disrespectful." Don't get emotional, don't lecture, just set the standard and move on.

When drafting in-the-moment feedback, structure it as:
- **What happened** (concrete, observable, no interpretation)
- **What impact it had** (on the team, the project, the person on the receiving end)
- **What you'd like instead** (specific, behavioral)
- **An invitation to respond** — feedback is a conversation, not a verdict

## Continuous feedback planning

If the user wants to build the habit, walk them through Fournier's three steps:

1. **Know your people.** For each direct report, write down: their current level, their goals (if any), their strengths, their development areas. Use prior reviews if you have them. If you don't know any of these for someone, that's your first 1-1 topic.

2. **Observe them.** The skill encourages the user to set a weekly goal: identify one specific thing each report did that deserves praise. Not "good job on the project" — something specific. This forces the user to actually pay attention. It also catches the failure mode of obsessing over problems while ignoring strengths.

3. **Provide lightweight, regular feedback.** Mostly positive, specific, in the moment. Save corrective feedback for when there's a real pattern or a real example. If the user has the bandwidth, add coaching — but coaching is a bonus, not the baseline.

## Review writing

When the user is writing a review, walk them through this sequence. Don't skip steps.

### 1. Set yourself up

- Block real time. Reviews can't be knocked out in an hour.
- Read all the collected 360 feedback first. Take notes. Don't try to write the summary on first pass.
- Pull up your 1-1 notes and any feedback you've delivered through the year. This is where the "whole year, not just the last two months" rule lives.

### 2. Structure the review

Use this template unless the user's company mandates a different one:

```
# Performance Review: [Name], [Period]

## Summary
2–3 sentences. What's the headline? Are they exceeding, meeting, or below
expectations? What's the one thing that defines their year?

## Accomplishments and strengths
This is the longest section. Don't skimp here even if it feels easier to focus on
gaps. Concrete examples. Cite peer reviews (anonymized if company process requires).

## Areas for growth / development
One to three themes max. Each tied to a concrete example. If you have
scattershot feedback from peers with no theme, use judgment — don't dump
every grudge into the review.

## Goals for next period
Specific, time-boxed, tied to the growth areas.
```

### 3. Watch for the bias traps

Before sending the review to anyone, ask:

- **Recency bias** — Am I overweighting the last 2 months because that's what I remember? Check 1-1 notes from earlier in the period.
- **Halo / horns** — One big thing they did well (or badly) is coloring everything else. Make sure each section can stand on its own examples.
- **Scattershot feedback from peers** — One reviewer says "sloppy work" and no one else does. Is the work sloppy, or does that one reviewer have higher standards than the team? Use judgment; don't pass every grudge through.
- **The "potential" trap** — "He has potential" without performance to match is often bias. Fournier: real potential shows up as going the extra mile, insightful suggestions, helping in neglected areas — not as credentials or charm.

### 4. Delivery

- Hand them a printed copy the evening before, so they can read it once before the meeting. This prevents the conversation being them reading silently while you sit there.
- Walk through each section. Don't let them skip the strengths section to obsess over the gaps. Many people will try.
- If there's a numeric rating less than top, come prepared with concrete examples of what a higher rating would look like.
- **No surprises.** If anything in this review will be new news to the person, you have a delivery problem, not a content problem — and you need to flag it as such. Consider: is this fair to deliver in a review, or should it have come up months ago?

## Pre-review surprise check

If the user is heading into review season and wants to make sure nothing will surprise anyone:

For each direct report, work through:
1. What rating / level are they likely to get?
2. Have they been told things consistent with that rating throughout the year?
3. Is there any corrective feedback in the review they have not yet heard from you in a 1-1?
4. Is there any praise in the review you haven't already given them?

Anything in column 3 or 4 needs a 1-1 conversation **before** the review is delivered. Not the day before — actual prior conversation.

## On firing and PIPs (performance improvement plans)

If review writing reveals that someone is genuinely below expectations:

- Most companies require a PIP before firing. Follow your HR process.
- **The rule of no surprises applies hardest here.** If a PIP is the first time someone is hearing they're underperforming, you (or their previous manager) failed.
- Keep written records of negative feedback. Even with no HR — email is enough.
- Fournier's warning: don't put anyone on a PIP whom you wouldn't be happy to lose. Smart employees treat a surprise PIP as the signal to leave, and the goodwill goes with them.

This crosses into hard-conversation territory — if the user is in this zone, suggest they also consult the hard-conversations skill.

## Forcing functions

When generating review content, the skill should:

- **Refuse vague feedback.** "Communication could be better" is not feedback. Push for the specific incident, the specific impact, the specific behavior to change.
- **Refuse to bury bad news.** If the user is hedging, name it: "It sounds like you're trying to soften 'this person isn't ready for promotion' — they deserve to hear that clearly with the reasoning."
- **Refuse to skip strengths.** If a draft is 20% strengths and 80% gaps, flag it. Even underperformers have strengths; not naming them makes the review feel like an attack rather than an assessment.
- **Surface implied promotions.** If the review is "they're crushing it at everything," ask: is it time to promote? If not, why not? People who are unambiguously exceeding their level either get promoted or eventually leave.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't write reviews from thin context. If the user gives you two sentences and asks for a review draft, push back and ask for examples, 1-1 notes, peer feedback.
- Don't generate corporate-HR-speak. Fournier's tone is direct and human. Match that.
- Don't suggest the user "have a difficult conversation" without helping them prepare for it. That's a copout.
