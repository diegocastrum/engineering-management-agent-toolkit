# Hard Conversations

Source: Camille Fournier, *The Manager's Path*, chapters 4–8 (firing, saying no, conflict, delivering bad news, the "Conflict Avoider, Conflict Tamer" pattern).

The thread that runs through everything Fournier says about hard conversations: **kind, not nice**. Nice is the language of polite strangers. Kind is what's actually useful, and it's often what feels uncomfortable.

## When this skill is loaded, identify the conversation type

The mechanics differ a lot. Match the situation:

- **Performance / firing-adjacent** — PIP, coaching out, telling someone they're not meeting expectations
- **Promotion no** — they want it, they're not ready, you have to tell them
- **Saying no upward** — pushing back on your boss or leadership on a project / deadline / reorg
- **Saying no to a peer** — another team's manager wants something from your team
- **Saying no to your own team** — declining a request from one of your reports
- **Behavior correction** — addressing a brilliant jerk, a noncommunicator, disrespect, or similar
- **Bad news at scale** — layoffs, reorg, project cancellation, big policy change

Then apply the relevant pattern below.

## Performance and firing-adjacent

These are the conversations where the **no-surprises rule** is most important. If the conversation will be a surprise, that's a problem you have to address before the conversation, not in it.

### Prep questions

Before the conversation, the user should be able to answer:

1. **What specifically is the gap?** "Not performing" is not enough. What level/role is this person supposed to be operating at, and what observable behaviors aren't happening?
2. **What feedback have you given this person before, and when?** If the answer is "not much," stop. You need to give that feedback first, in 1-1s, with a real chance to improve, before formal action.
3. **Is the gap fixable on this team?** Sometimes the person could thrive elsewhere — coaching out (helping them transition to another team or company) is more humane than firing.
4. **Have you talked to HR / your manager?** Required at most companies.
5. **What's your timeline?** Improvement plan first (formal or informal), then decision.

### The conversation itself

Structure:

```
Opening: state the purpose clearly. No small talk that delays.
"I want to talk to you about [the gap]. This is serious and I want to be direct."

Specifics: what's happening, what's not happening, with examples.
"In the last quarter, you've [example 1]. I expected [what should have happened].
This is a pattern I've seen with [example 2] and [example 3]."

Impact: why this matters.
"This affects [the team / the project / the relationship with X]."

Their turn: invite response. Listen.
"I want to hear how you're seeing this."

Path forward: clear, concrete, time-bound.
"For the next [period], I need to see [specific behaviors / outcomes]. We'll
meet weekly to review progress. If we don't see [specific bar] by [date],
we'll need to talk about whether this role is the right fit."
```

### Coaching out

Fournier's term for the case where someone has plateaued and a graceful exit is the kindness. Different from firing — the person isn't failing at their current level, they just can't grow to the next level on this team, and they're getting frustrated.

The conversation:
- Acknowledge what they're good at and what they've contributed
- Be direct: "I've told you what the next level looks like; the gap hasn't closed. I don't think this team is the right place for you to grow further."
- Frame it as their option: "I'm not firing you. I'm telling you I'd help you find a fit elsewhere if you want."
- Don't make ultimatums about timeline unless their performance is also slipping.

### The "do not put on a PIP" rule

Fournier's specific warning: if you wouldn't be happy to lose this person, **don't put them on a PIP**. A surprise PIP is read by competent employees as "the company has decided." They leave quickly with goodwill destroyed. PIPs are for cases where you've already decided you can lose this person — they're a formal documentation step, not a recovery strategy.

## Promotion no

Telling someone they're not getting promoted when they expected to is one of the most common hard conversations. The principles:

- **Don't surprise them.** If they were lobbying for promotion this cycle and you knew they weren't ready, you should have said so weeks ago. If you didn't, your job now is to apologize for that gap and still be clear.
- **Be concrete about the bar.** Don't say "you need more leadership." Say "to be promoted to senior engineer here, the bar is X, Y, Z. You're doing well on X but I haven't seen Y or Z, and here are examples of what those look like."
- **Don't leave a vague "next time."** "Maybe next cycle" without specifics is worse than a clean no. Specify what would need to be true.
- **Don't promise what you can't deliver.** If promotions are committee-decided, you don't get to guarantee outcomes. Say what's in your control vs. what isn't.

## Saying no upward

Fournier's "Yes, and..." technique from chapter 6. The structure:

```
Yes, [we can do that / I understand that's the priority],
and [here's the trade-off / here's what would need to change].
```

Examples:
- "Yes, we can take on Project Z this quarter, and to do that we'll need to push Project Y to Q3 or get two more engineers."
- "Yes, I hear that the deadline is fixed, and to hit it we'll need to cut features A and B from the launch."
- "Yes, I understand this is the top priority, and we're currently 60% allocated to last quarter's top priority — we need to explicitly decide what stops."

The point isn't to refuse. It's to make the trade-off visible. Leaders making decisions with full information will sometimes still pick the path you'd have refused — that's their call. Your job is to make sure they're not picking it accidentally.

### When "yes, and" isn't enough

Some pushback needs to be flat:
- The work is unethical or violates policies
- The team will burn out catastrophically
- The proposed approach has technical consequences leadership doesn't understand

In these cases, you owe leadership a clear "I think this is a mistake, and here's why" — once. Then if they overrule you, you commit to delivering (Fournier's "disagree and commit"). What you can't do is openly disagree to your team after the decision is made.

## Saying no to a peer

When another team's manager wants something from your team:

- **Create policies** for repeated requests. "We don't take ad-hoc requests; here's our intake process."
- **Help me say yes** — when there's no clear policy: "I'd love to help. To make that work, I'd need to know X, Y, Z." Often this surfaces that their request isn't actually well-defined.
- **Appeal to budget** when it's a capacity issue. "We're at 100% allocation on committed projects this quarter. I can put it on the Q3 list if you want."
- **Work as a team** with cross-functional peers. Sometimes you and the product lead together saying no carries more weight than either alone.

## Saying no to your own team

When a report asks for something you have to decline:
- Don't prevaricate. "Let me think about it" is fine once. Twice is avoidance.
- Be direct and brief. "No, we can't do X right now because Y."
- Be honest about whether "right now" means "ever" or "not this cycle."
- Make it clear what *would* make the answer yes, if anything.

## Behavior correction

The brilliant jerk, the noncommunicator, the employee who lacks respect — see the team-health-debugger skill for diagnosis. For the conversation itself:

```
Open: state the issue with specific examples.
"In yesterday's design review, you said [exact quote]. In Tuesday's standup,
you [behavior]. I want to talk about the pattern."

Don't soften with "you might not have realized" if you think they did realize.
That's nice, not kind.

Impact: name it.
"When you talk to people that way, they stop bringing up dissenting views.
The team is missing out on their input because of how you respond."

Standard: clear.
"I need you to [specific behavior change]."

Acknowledge cost: this isn't easy if they've been rewarded for the behavior.
"I know this is a shift from how you've been operating. I'm telling you because
the behavior is causing real damage to the team."

Path: not a single conversation.
"We're going to revisit this in our 1-1s. If I don't see change in [period],
we'll need to escalate."
```

### Public correction (rare exception)

Fournier upends the "praise public, criticize private" rule once: when behavior is publicly damaging the team in real time, address it in the moment, neutrally:

> "Please don't speak to people that way; it's disrespectful."

Then move on. Don't lecture. Don't get emotional. The follow-up happens in private.

## Bad news at scale (layoffs, reorgs, cancellations)

The mechanics matter a lot here. Common mistakes:

- ❌ Mass email to a whole org
- ❌ Big all-hands meeting where the news is dropped on everyone simultaneously and the loudest reactions set the tone
- ❌ Letting the news leak before you've talked to the people most affected

What works:

- ✅ Talk to the people who'll react strongest first, individually
- ✅ Brief your management team, give them talking points, let them tell their reports
- ✅ Then convene the wider group to confirm what's been said and answer questions
- ✅ Be honest about outcomes. Acknowledge the pain. Don't sugarcoat.
- ✅ If you can't stand behind the message yourself, bring in someone who can co-deliver — another exec, HR.

### Honesty rule

Don't pretend to support a decision you violently disagree with. Either commit and deliver it, or step aside. Half-hearted delivery to your team — visible disagreement with leadership while still being the messenger — is the worst option.

## Prep template (use for any of the above)

When the user is prepping a specific conversation, walk them through:

```
1. The single sentence: in one sentence, what are you saying?

2. The opening: how do you start? (No small talk that delays the point.)

3. The specifics: what concrete examples / data / context do you have?

4. The path forward: what happens after this conversation? What do you
   need from them? What can they expect from you?

5. The objections you anticipate: what will they say back, and what's your
   response?

6. Your own emotional state: are you calm enough to do this well? If not,
   reschedule or get a co-deliverer.
```

Then have the user role-play it out loud (or in writing) before the real thing.

## Forcing functions

When helping with any hard conversation:

- **If the user is hedging language**, push back. "Soften this" usually means "make it less true." If the message is hard, the words should be clear.
- **If this is a surprise to the recipient**, name that as the bigger problem. Help them deliver the message, but also help them think about what feedback should have come earlier.
- **If the user wants to avoid the conversation entirely**, ask what the cost of avoidance is. Usually it's growing.
- **If the user is venting about the person before the conversation**, separate the venting from the prep. Vent first (briefly), then prep cleanly without the emotional charge.
- **If the user is asking the skill to write a script they'll read verbatim**, push back. Scripts read as scripts. Help them prepare key sentences but encourage them to have the conversation as a conversation.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't write HR-template language. Fournier's voice is direct and human; match that.
- Don't generate "feedback sandwich" structures. Burying corrective feedback between praise dilutes it.
- Don't suggest the user delay a needed conversation unless there's a real reason to wait.
- Don't help the user be cruel. Direct and kind are not the same as harsh.
