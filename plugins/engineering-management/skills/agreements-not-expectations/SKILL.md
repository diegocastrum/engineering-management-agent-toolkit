# Agreements, Not Expectations

Source: Niels Horeman, *From Assumptions to Agreements* (Leadership Essentials #5/7) and the companion *Playbook — Creating and Honoring Agreements*.

Most workplace friction traces to **unspoken expectations** rather than missing skill, will, or talent. Horeman's argument: every interaction sits on one of two foundations — *expectations* (vague, one-sided, often unspoken) or *agreements* (explicit, mutual, voluntary, action-based). The discipline is to shift more of the conversation to the second.

## When this skill is loaded

Match what the user is doing:

- **Diagnostic** — something failed; was it really a failure, or just an unspoken expectation?
- **Creating** — setting up a new agreement (with a report, peer, vendor, partner, team)
- **Out-of-agreement** — something broke; what to say and do
- **Modeling** — the user wants to set the tone for a team or leadership group
- **Reframing** — the user is in blame mode and needs to redirect to clarity mode

## The core distinction

| Expectation | Agreement |
|---|---|
| Often unspoken | Explicit and spoken aloud |
| One-sided | Mutual |
| Vague | Specific and action-based |
| Assumed | Voluntarily chosen |
| Produces frustration, blame, rework | Produces clarity, trust, workability |
| "They should have known" | "We agreed X by Wednesday noon" |

**The test:** *If someone can't remember it later, it wasn't an agreement — it was an expectation.*

## The five principles of agreements

Every real agreement satisfies all five. If one is missing, you have an expectation in disguise.

### 1. Action-based, not outcome-based

Agreements describe *what someone will do*, not *what result will appear*. Actions are within control; outcomes are influenced by external factors.

- ✗ "Sales will hit the quarterly target."
- ✓ "Sales will make 50 outbound prospect calls per week."
- ✗ "We'll look into it."
- ✓ "Marketing delivers the draft by Wednesday noon, and Product confirms in the project channel."

If you can't observe whether it happened, it's not action-based.

### 2. Formal and spoken

Silence is not agreement. Write it down, repeat it back, confirm alignment.

- The agreement exists in a place both parties can find it later — Slack message, doc, ticket, email.
- One party paraphrases the agreement back; the other confirms.
- Nodding in a meeting is not consent. Get the words.

### 3. Voluntary

A real agreement is freely chosen. No pressure, no manipulation, no implied threat. The only meaningful yes is a voluntary yes.

There's a nuance: *participation* in a context (a team, a meeting, a project) is voluntary. Once you've stepped in, the agreements that hold the container apply — see "entry conditions" below.

If someone agreed because they felt they couldn't say no, you don't have an agreement. You have compliance. Compliance breaks the first time it's tested.

### 4. Mutual responsibility: 100/100, not 50/50

Both parties take *full* responsibility for the agreement working. This isn't 50/50 split-the-load — it's 100/100, both fully in.

Concretely:
- The doer takes responsibility for delivering.
- The other party takes responsibility for noticing if it's at risk, offering support, checking in, removing blockers.
- Neither waits silently for the other to fail.

This is sometimes confused with micromanagement. It isn't. Micromanagement is controlling *how*. 100/100 is shared ownership of *whether it happens at all*.

### 5. Exitable and renegotiable

Circumstances change. Agreements can be updated, exited, or replaced — *formally, with both parties*. Renegotiation is not failure; it's how the system stays current.

What's not allowed: silently dropping an agreement. That's where integrity breaks.

## Entry conditions: the agreements that hold the container

Some agreements aren't preferences — they're conditions for the group to function. They're voluntary at the level of participation (you can choose not to join), not at the level of content (once in, they apply).

Common entry conditions for leadership groups:

- **Presence.** No Slack, no phone, no multitasking. If you can't give full attention, step out. Distraction undermines the whole.
- **Confidentiality.** What's shared in the room stays there. Insights can cascade outward; names and specifics don't.
- **Time.** Meetings start and end on time. Respect for time is respect for the group.

These aren't rules imposed from above. They're structures that protect the integrity of the whole. Frame them that way when introducing them.

## Integrity: being your word

Agreements are nothing without integrity. Integrity isn't perfection — it's *being your word*.

Two halves:

- **Keeping your word** — doing what you said you would do.
- **Being your word** — communicating and recommitting when you can't keep it.

A missed deadline with no communication erodes trust. A missed deadline with proactive, clear communication *can strengthen it* — the group feels the commitment even in absence.

Leaders set tone. If a senior person shows up late without acknowledgment, the whole group feels it. Naming it preserves the culture:

> "We're out of agreement on time, and I apologize."

Integrity is restored not by being perfect, but by naming it.

## The out-of-agreement script

When an agreement isn't held, use this language. It's deliberately simple and non-blaming. The pattern is **name → take responsibility → recommit or renegotiate**.

### Step 1: Name it

> "We're out of agreement about X."

That's it. No softening, no editorial, no story. Just locate the break.

### Step 2: Take responsibility

> "I said I'd deliver it Monday and didn't."

No "but," no excuses, no blame trail. Just the fact that the agreement wasn't kept.

### Step 3: Then either recommit *or* renegotiate

**Recommit** — the agreement still applies, you'll just hit it differently:

> "I will deliver it before Thursday at 11am."

**Renegotiate** — the agreement itself doesn't work anymore:

> "This agreement isn't working for me. Can we create a new one?"

What's *not* on the script: blame, guilt, story, justification. The language is for clarity and recreation, not for processing emotion.

## Reframes the user may need

### "Isn't this micromanaging?"

No. Micromanagement controls *how* work gets done. 100/100 responsibility is shared ownership of *whether the agreement holds*. Curiosity, check-ins, and support are not control. They're the other side's half of the agreement.

### "Doesn't this clash with our culture of freedom?"

No. Agreements *protect* freedom. Without agreements you get either chaos (freedom without responsibility) or control (responsibility without freedom). Inside agreements, freedom and responsibility reinforce each other — autonomy is guaranteed *and* workability is preserved.

### "Aren't we supposed to keep agreements perfectly?"

No. The goal is to *see where they don't hold*. Broken agreements are data — they locate weak points in the system. Think of it like debugging: a broken agreement points to where clarity, responsibility, or integrity needs strengthening. Not shameful — just diagnostic.

### "How does the team feel about this?"

Wrong question. Ask *"Is this system workable?"* instead. Someone may individually feel fine multitasking in a meeting — but collectively, workability breaks. Energy drops, decisions weaken. Prioritize the system over individual preference.

### "I assumed it was obvious."

That's the tell. *Obvious to me* is not an agreement. It's an expectation. Naming your own slip ("That was my expectation, not our agreement") is high-leverage — it shows the team this is a shared discipline, not a control tool.

## Systems beat willpower

Relying on willpower to honor agreements is a setup for failure. Willpower fluctuates; systems don't.

Concrete examples:
- A "weekly check-in" that lives only in your head will be missed. The same check-in on a recurring calendar invite will hold.
- A team commitment to "ship faster" without a measurable cadence will drift. A commitment to "PRs reviewed within 24 hours, tracked in the team dashboard" won't.
- A personal commitment to "be more present in 1-1s" without a structural change (closing the laptop, no phone, dedicated room) will erode.

When an agreement keeps breaking, *the fix is usually structural, not motivational*. Build the system around the agreement.

## Creating an agreement (the process)

When the user is setting one up:

1. **Discussion.** Both parties surface needs and capabilities. What does each side actually need from this?
2. **Action-language.** Translate the vague version into specific observable actions. ("Be more responsive" → "Reply within 4 business hours during weekdays.")
3. **Voluntary check.** Is this a real yes, or a polite yes? If unsure, name it: "I want to make sure this works for you — say no if it doesn't."
4. **Mutual responsibility.** What does each side commit to? Including the support and check-in side, not just the doing side.
5. **Documentation.** Where does this live? Calendar invite, Slack message in a project channel, doc, ticket.
6. **Exit clause.** What happens if circumstances change? Default: either party can call for renegotiation any time.
7. **System support.** Is there a structure that makes keeping this agreement automatic, or is it depending on memory and willpower? If the latter, add structure.

## Output format

For **creating an agreement**, produce a structured draft the user can use directly:

```
Agreement between [A] and [B]

What [A] will do: [specific action(s), with timing]
What [B] will do: [specific action(s), with timing — including check-ins / support]

Where this lives: [calendar / doc / Slack channel / ticket]
Review point: [date or trigger]
Exit clause: Either party can call for renegotiation at any time.
```

For **out-of-agreement repair**, produce the actual message or words to use, in the name → responsibility → recommit/renegotiate pattern.

For **diagnostic mode**, walk through:

1. Was there an actual agreement, or just an expectation?
2. If agreement: which of the 5 principles was missing?
3. If expectation: how would you turn this into an agreement going forward?
4. What's the system change that would prevent this category of break in future?

## Forcing functions

- **If the user is in blame mode** ("they should have...", "I can't believe they..."), redirect to the diagnostic question: *was there actually an agreement, or just an expectation?*
- **If the user says "we agreed"** but can't recall when, where, or in what words, that's the test — it wasn't an agreement.
- **If a proposed agreement is outcome-based** ("hit the target", "improve the metric"), push for the actions underneath that are actually in someone's control.
- **If a yes feels reluctant**, name it. A polite yes that's actually a no breaks the first time it's tested. Better to surface the no now.
- **If the user is about to silently let an agreement drop**, push for the explicit out-of-agreement conversation. Silence is the integrity break, not the miss itself.
- **If an agreement keeps breaking**, stop trying harder. Look for the structural fix — calendar, system, who-owns-what — not the willpower fix.
- **If the user is hedging an apology** ("sorry, but..."), the "but" is the problem. The script is *name → responsibility → recommit*, without the editorial.
- **If the user is asking "how does everyone feel about this?"**, suggest "is this workable?" instead. Different question, different answer.
- **If the user just realized they were operating on an unspoken expectation**, the move is to name it: "That was my expectation, not our agreement." That single sentence rebuilds the relationship.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't moralize. The frame is *workable / not workable*, not *right / wrong*.
- Don't generate scripts that pressure someone into agreeing. A coerced yes isn't an agreement.
- Don't treat broken agreements as character failures. They're system failures, and the fix is structural.
- Don't push this language into casual contexts where it would land as cult-coachy. ("We're out of agreement about the lunch order" is wrong.) Use it for things that matter.
- Don't use the framework to avoid hard conversations — it's a tool *for* having them clearly, not a way to outsource them to process.
