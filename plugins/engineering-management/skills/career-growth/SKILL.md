# Career Growth

Sources:
- Camille Fournier, *The Manager's Path*, ch. 4 ("Cultivating Careers"), ch. 9 ("Writing a Career Ladder"), and the threads on managing yourself throughout.
- James Stanier, *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, ch. 18 ("The Crystal Ball").

Three distinct things live here:
1. **Growing your team members' careers** — your job as a manager.
2. **Growing your own** — your job as a person.
3. **Annual deep-dive vision work** — a once-a-year structured exercise, deeper than the next promotion, that doesn't fit weekly 1-1s or 6-month reviews.

The skill handles all three.

## When this skill is loaded — identify mode

- **Theirs (a report's)** — "X wants to be a tech lead, how do I help?" / "Is Y ready for promotion?"
- **Mine (the user's)** — "I want to grow into a director role" / "Should I take this management track?"
- **Structural** — designing or evolving the ladder itself, calibration across the team, equity in promotions.
- **Annual vision exercise** — once-a-year structured deep dive; multiple sessions; surfaces a 10-year vision and a 1-year skills backlog. See *Annual vision exercise* near the end.

Ask if not clear.

## Growing a report's career

The conversation starts with this question — and the user should be able to answer it about every direct report:

> **What does this person want, and what do they need to learn to get there?**

If you don't know, your next 1-1 has a topic. Use prompts like:
- "Where do you want to be in 2 years? 5 years?"
- "What kinds of work do you find most energizing?"
- "Have you thought about whether you want to manage people eventually?"
- "What's a project that would feel like a real stretch right now?"

People often don't have crisp answers. That's fine — surface the rough shape and revisit. For people who say "I don't know where I want to go," that's the signal for the annual vision exercise.

### Identifying promotion readiness

For each report, periodically (every quarter or two) ask:

1. **What's their current level?**
2. **What's the next level?** What's actually different — skills, scope, impact, leadership?
3. **Are they currently exhibiting some of the next-level behaviors?** Many companies require "acting at the next level before being promoted to it" to avoid the Peter Principle.
4. **What's the gap?** Specific behaviors / experiences / impact they need to demonstrate.
5. **What's the path?** What project, what scope expansion, what mentorship would close the gap?

### Early-career promotions (E2 → E3, etc.)

For people in their first 1–3 years out of school:

- Promotion is usually default, *unless something is wrong*. Companies often have "up or out" policies at these levels.
- Your job is to **not get in their way** more than it is to push them forward.
- Make sure they're learning to estimate their work, getting it done within the estimates, learning from mistakes, participating in on-call, engaging in team planning.

### Mid-career to senior (E3 → senior, senior → staff)

This is where promotions get harder and slower. Fewer opportunities to demonstrate the kind of leadership / scope / impact required.

Your job:

- **Identify promotion-worthy projects** — work that would naturally produce the artifacts a promotion case needs (cross-team impact, technical leadership, project leadership).
- **Assign them** to people close to promotion, or encourage those people to volunteer.
- **Teach them how the game is played** at your company. Transparency about the promotion process is itself a fairness move. People who understand the criteria don't waste effort on the wrong things.
- **Stretch projects** are the lever. The right project at the right time is what produces the promotion case.

### Senior to staff and beyond

These promotions are no longer "up or out." A senior engineer can stay senior forever and that's fine. Same for senior managers.

For people who want to advance further:

- Senior promotions usually require **scope** that the current team may not provide.
- Sometimes the answer is moving to a different team or a different problem space.
- Tech leads, mentors, and architectural contributors at this level demonstrate leadership through influence, not authority — the promotion case is harder to assemble.

### When someone's stuck

> From Fournier — *Coaching Someone Out of the Company* (ch. 4). Symptoms:

- Doing OK work but not progressing for a long period
- You've told them what the next level requires, multiple times
- They go back to their comfort zone every time
- They're losing energy / engagement

The kind move: be direct. "I've told you what the next level looks like, and I haven't seen the change. I don't think this team / company is where you'll grow next. I'd help you find a fit elsewhere."

This isn't firing. They're meeting expectations at their level. It's acknowledging that growth requires a different environment, and being honest rather than letting them stagnate.

(Crosses into hard-conversations territory — also consult that skill.)

## Career mentorship beyond promotion

Promotion is one form of growth. Others matter too:

- **Skill depth** — getting truly good at something they're already doing
- **Skill breadth** — new areas (frontend → backend, individual → leading, IC → management)
- **Visibility** — speaking, writing, open source, conference talks
- **Network** — connections within and outside the company

For each report, periodically ask: which of these are they actively investing in? Is there one that would compound?

### The mentorship vs. management distinction

Not every senior person wants to manage. Some want to stay deeply technical. Both paths are valid; your ladder should reflect that.

For senior ICs who don't want to manage but still want to grow:

- Mentorship of more junior engineers is part of the senior IC job, not optional.
- Project leadership without people-management is the typical scope expansion.
- Architectural / strategic impact starts to matter more than feature output.

## The user's own career

When the user is thinking about their own growth:

### The IC vs. management decision

Both tracks have real costs and rewards. Common traps:

- **Choosing management to avoid being "left behind"** when peers move up — bad reason. Many companies have IC ladders that go as high.
- **Choosing management because someone told you you'd be good at it** — fine, but make sure *you* want it. The job is harder than it looks and emphatically different from what made you a strong engineer.
- **Choosing IC because management seems unpleasant** — also fine, but recognize that staying technical past a point makes it harder to move into management later, not easier.

The decision isn't permanent. Switching back to IC after a management stint is common and valid.

### Growing as a manager

For someone already managing:

- **What level are you at?** (Team lead → multiple teams → managing managers → senior leadership)
- **What's the next level?** What's actually different about it?
- **Are you doing any of those things now?** Most growth from one management level to the next comes from voluntarily taking on responsibilities you don't technically have.

Specific growth moves at each level:

- **Team lead → managing multiple teams**: develop tech leads who can own their teams; learn to delegate complex frequent tasks; spend less time in code, more on strategy.
- **Multiple teams → managing managers**: practice skip-levels; learn to hold managers accountable rather than doing their jobs for them; develop instincts for organizational debugging.
- **Managing managers → senior leadership**: strategy becomes the main job; build relationships with cross-functional peers; learn to "disagree and commit."

### Self-management

The biggest growth lever at every level is managing yourself. Specifically:

- **Time management** — distinguishing important from urgent (most managers spend too much on urgent-but-unimportant).
- **Emotional regulation** — particularly around criticism, conflict, and ambiguity.
- **Curiosity** — defaulting to "tell me more" rather than judging or attacking.
- **Self-awareness** — knowing what triggers you, what drains you, what you're avoiding.

These aren't "soft skills." They're the multipliers on every other skill.

### Finding mentors and coaches

The further up you go, the less likely your manager is to be a great coach. Build external sources:

- A coach (paid, even if your company won't pay).
- Peers at your level at other companies (a small group, low-volume).
- Mentors who've been where you're going.
- Reading: Fournier recommends Andy Grove's *High Output Management*, Patrick Lencioni's *Five Dysfunctions*, Brené Brown's *Daring Greatly*, Marshall Goldsmith's *What Got You Here Won't Get You There*, L. David Marquet's *Turn the Ship Around*.

## Designing or evolving a career ladder

If the user is creating or rewriting a ladder, work through these (Fournier ch. 9):

1. **Don't write it alone.** Get senior engineers and managers to draft the parts that affect them. Their buy-in is essential.
2. **Look at examples.** Many companies publish their ladders now. Use them as starting points, not as templates to copy wholesale — what works for one company often fails for another.
3. **Be detailed.** Vague ladders create gaming. Specific behavioral descriptions reduce it.
4. **Two documents:**
   - Short spreadsheet for side-by-side comparison across levels.
   - Long-form descriptions that read like a performance review of someone at that level.
5. **Salary bands:**
   - Narrow bands and many levels early-career — supports frequent promotions and raises.
   - Wide bands and fewer levels at senior — accommodates strong performers who aren't ready for the next level.
6. **Breakpoint level**: define where "up or out" stops being a rule. Usually around senior engineer. Below: people advance or leave. At and above: indefinite tenure is fine.
7. **Keystone promotions**: certain promotions (senior, staff, principal, director, VP) should be recognized publicly. People need something to aim at beyond the next pay bump.
8. **Split management and IC tracks** above senior. Don't force people into management to advance.
9. **Mentorship as a requirement** for promotion above mid-career. Even on the IC track, you can't get to senior+ without having demonstrated leadership through mentorship or tech-leading.
10. **Years of experience**: don't use as a hard gate, but use as a sanity check. Staff engineers need maturity that takes time to develop.
11. **Expect to evolve it.** The first version will be wrong. Plan to revise within a year.

## Annual vision exercise (deep dive)

> From Stanier — most career conversations don't get past the next promotion. Spend a few hours, once a year, working through a structured exercise that produces a long-term vision and a concrete short-term plan. When delivered to a report, it becomes a series of coaching sessions, not a single meeting.

### When to do this exercise

- **Once a year**, after performance reviews (so growth feedback feeds in).
- **After a major life change** for the report (new role, new manager, life event).
- **When a report says they "don't know where they want to go"** — that's the signal.

Not for every weekly 1-1. This is a deeper exercise, done deliberately. Different cadence from `one-on-ones` (weekly) and `feedback-and-reviews` (6-month).

### The six steps

When delivering to a report, this becomes a sequence of coaching sessions:

1. **Past timeline** — looking back over 10 years.
2. **10-year coaching session** — using the past to dream the future.
3. **Vision statement** — refining the vision into a document.
4. **Input, scoring, homework** — peer feedback on competencies + draft plan.
5. **One-year coaching session** — building the skills backlog.
6. **Ongoing review** — quarterly check-ins on the backlog.

For self-use, the user does each step alone (no coaching session needed; they're coaching themselves).

### Step 1 — Look back ten years

Draw a horizontal line representing the last 10 years. Plot significant events — career *and* personal:

- Jobs, roles, promotions
- Education, training
- Geographic moves
- Major projects
- Personal milestones (relationships, family, losses)

The point: most people radically underestimate how much can happen in 10 years. Looking at the actual filled-out timeline expands the horizon for the future.

After plotting, reflect on what played a role in each event. Five categories:

- **Training** — formal education, courses, self-directed learning.
- **Luck** — being in the right place; meeting the right person.
- **People** — mentors, advocates, peers who shaped trajectory.
- **Forethought** — decisions made deliberately with the future in mind.
- **Hindsight** — lessons learned that you wish you'd applied earlier.

This pass tunes you to recognize that the next 10 years will involve all five — and you can influence training, forethought, and (to some extent) people deliberately.

### Step 2 — Coach toward a 10-year vision

The heart of the exercise. The trap: people aim too low — they describe the next 2 years and call it a vision.

If running this with a report, your job is to **coach, not direct**. Keep the thought bubble over their head. Ask provocative questions to push past the safe answers.

Prompts that move people to think bigger:

- How do you feel about your current job? What do you enjoy *most*? What do you wish you could change?
- How do your skills map to the most innovative work in industry right now? Is that where you want to be heading?
- Are there mornings you can't wait to get to work? Why?
- Are there mornings you can't bear lifting the covers? Why?
- *Imagine yourself waking up 10 years from now.* What does the bedroom look like? Where do you live? What do you see out the window? You make breakfast — who's there? You get dressed and look in the mirror — what are you wearing? Where are you going, and how? Embody that future person.

Listen for understatement. "I want to work in product" might actually mean "I want to be the CPO at my own successful startup." Probe.

### Step 3 — Write the vision statement

After the coaching session, the person writes their vision as a structured document:

```markdown
# My Vision

## 10-year vision
[Compelling first-person statements. "I will be [role] at [type of company]
in [type of place]. We will [mission]. I will be living in [location] with
[family/situation]."]

## Most rewarding achievements in the last 12 months
[What's been most rewarding and how does it support the future vision?]

## What excites me most about my current role
[How does that excitement project forward?]

## Do I see myself managing people in the future?
[If yes — what kind? What do you like about it?]

## Formal qualifications and their role
[Do current qualifications matter? Do I need more?]
```

The "I will" language matters. Make the vision feel touchable.

**Get external pressure-test** after writing:

- Send to partner, close friends, trusted family. Realistic? Overambitious? Underambitious?
- Find five people whose current state matches your 10-year vision. Look at their profiles. How did they get there? What do they talk about? Who do they interact with?

### Step 4 — Review and refine

Schedule another session after they've drafted.

- Read it together. Does it resonate now that time has passed?
- Is it actually a *vision* (compelling, magnetic) or a list of tasks?
- What needs sharpening, what feels generic?
- Is it underambitious — does it still just describe the next promotion?

Probe for what's missing. Refine.

### Step 5 — From vision to skills backlog

Now move from 10 years to the next year. Backward-chain:

- What needs to be true in 5 years to reach the 10-year vision?
- What needs to be true in 3 years?
- What needs to be true in 2 years?
- What needs to be true in 1 year?
- What do I need to do in the next 6 months?

The 1-year answer becomes the **plan statement**. Concrete and measurable.

```markdown
# My Plan

This year, I need to [primary focus statement].

I know I struggle with [X]. I need to practice this by [specific action].

I need to become confident at [Y]. I can do this by [specific behavior].

I don't know enough people in [Z]. I need to [specific outreach].

I'll know I've succeeded this year if [observable outcome].
```

**Score current competencies.** Using the career ladder (or see the `motivation-and-fit` skill), rate yourself on each competency at your current level. Get peer feedback on the same competencies from 3–5 colleagues to triangulate. Gaps between your self-rating and others' ratings are discussion topics — sometimes you're harder on yourself, sometimes others see something you don't.

**Build the skills backlog.** A Trello-style board or simple list. Each card = a skill or competency to practice with concrete actions.

| Backlog | Doing | Done |
|---|---|---|
| Run 10 1-1s using contracting | Lead my first hiring interview | Wrote first ADR |
| Give a department talk | Learning to write structured weekly summary | Audited my calendar |
| Mentor a junior on Go | ... | ... |

Each card has comments where you record progress. Review the board regularly.

### Step 6 — Ongoing review

- **Quarterly:** check the backlog with the report. What's moved? What hasn't? Why?
- **Every 6 months:** revisit the plan statement. Still right? Adjust.
- **Annually:** redo the full exercise. The vision evolves; that's normal.

When delivering to a report:

```markdown
# Session [N]: [topic]

## Goal
[What this session achieves]

## Coaching questions to use

## Likely sticking points and how to handle
- ...

## Homework after this session
- ...
```

## Forcing functions

- **If the user can't name what the next level requires** for a report — fix that first. You can't help someone get promoted to a level you can't define.
- **If the user has been telling a report "soon" for multiple cycles** without it happening — name the gap. Soon-without-specifics is unkind.
- **If the user is debating their own management transition** and waffling — ask what *they* want, not what their boss wants for them. Pressure into management without desire is a setup for misery.
- **If a high performer hasn't been promoted in a long time** — investigate. Either the level expectations aren't actually being met (and they're not really high-performing at the next level), or you're losing them.
- **If "everyone deserves a promotion" pressure** is mounting — push back. Promotions are signals of operating at a new level, not seniority rewards. Inflating them dilutes the meaning.
- **If a vision exercise reads like a job description for "next role"**, push to expand the horizon. 10 years is the unit.
- **If the user (or report) skips the past-timeline exercise**, encourage doing it. The expansion of what's possible comes from seeing how much actually happened in 10 years.
- **If a plan statement has no measurable outcome**, refine. "Become a better manager" isn't measurable; "lead 4 successful hires and run 10 effective 1-1s on contracting" is.
- **If the user is delivering the vision exercise to a report and starts directing rather than coaching**, redirect. The vision has to come from the report, not from the manager.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't pretend career paths are linear. They're not.
- Don't recommend ambition for ambition's sake. Some people thrive at their level and that's fine.
- Don't help the user write a vague "growth plan" with no concrete projects or behaviors. That's a checkbox, not a plan.
- Don't generate corporate-speak. Career conversations are between two humans.
- Don't generate a generic vision statement for the user. The exercise depends on the work *they* do; predigested visions don't stick.
- Don't push reports to align their vision with the company's needs. Sometimes the right vision means leaving — that's fine and useful to know.
- Don't compress the vision exercise into a single meeting. It's a sequence over time. Compressing it kills the work that happens between sessions.
