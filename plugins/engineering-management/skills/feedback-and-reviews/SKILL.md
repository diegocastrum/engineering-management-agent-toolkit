# Feedback and Performance Reviews

Sources:
- James Stanier, *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, ch. 6 ("The Most Wonderful Time of the Year")
- Camille Fournier, *The Manager's Path*, ch. 4 ("Creating a Culture of Continuous Feedback" and "Performance Reviews")

The core rule that organizes everything in this skill: **no surprises**. If feedback first appears in a performance review, you failed earlier. Continuous feedback is the day-to-day discipline; reviews are the periodic synthesis. Neither works without the other.

## When this skill is loaded — identify mode

- **In-the-moment feedback** — something just happened (good or bad) and the user is deciding whether/how to say something.
- **Continuous feedback planning** — user wants to build the habit; nothing specific has happened yet.
- **Review setup** — installing the review process from scratch (cadence, tracker, peer feedback).
- **Peer feedback mode** — soliciting and collating peer input.
- **Review writing** — drafting the written form before the meeting.
- **Pre-review surprise check** — review season is coming and the user wants to make sure nothing will surprise anyone.
- **Review meeting** — running the meeting itself.
- **Pay conversation** — handling the salary discussion.
- **PIP / firing zone** — review reveals someone is genuinely below expectations.
- **Myth-busting mode** — user has misconceptions about what reviews are for.

## The seven myths to bust

> From Stanier — if the user voices any of these, address it first; everything else assumes they're past these.

1. **Reviews are top-down feedback.** Wrong — they're two-way. Both sides prepare and contribute.
2. **Reviews are just an HR thing.** Wrong — even if HR didn't make you do them, you should. They set the bar and course-correct.
3. **Reviews are mainly for underperformers.** Wrong — your top performers need them most. They're the ones with the most growth runway.
4. **Get them over quickly because people hate them.** Wrong — engage fully. People hate them because they're done badly.
5. **The review should be a surprise.** Wrong — almost everything in the review should already be known. The form is filled in *before* the meeting and both parties read it.
6. **Reviews unveil pay raises.** Wrong — decouple them. See *Pay* below.
7. **Reviews are the only place performance is discussed.** Wrong — performance is a year-round 1-1 topic. Reviews are a checkpoint, not the only checkpoint.

## In-the-moment feedback

If the user says "X did Y, should I say something?" — the answer is almost always yes, and almost always soon.

### Decision tree

1. **Is this praise?** Give it now, briefly, and in public if the person is OK with public praise (check what they said in contracting). The single biggest under-used lever for new managers is more frequent specific praise.

2. **Is this corrective?** Three sub-questions:
   - **One-off or pattern?** One-offs from someone with a good track record can be mentioned lightly or skipped. Patterns must be named.
   - **Did it affect the team or just irritate you?** If it's the team, address it. If it's just you, sit with it for 24 hours — sometimes it's your reaction, not their behavior.
   - **Can you tie it to a concrete example?** If yes, deliver soon (within a day or two), in private, calmly. If no, wait until you have one. Vague feedback is unfair.

3. **Is the behavior public and harmful?** (e.g., someone being rude to a colleague in a meeting.) Rare exception to "praise in public, criticize in private" — address in the moment, neutrally and briefly: "Please don't speak to people that way; it's disrespectful." Don't get emotional, don't lecture, just set the standard and move on.

### Structure for delivering it

- **What happened** — concrete, observable, no interpretation.
- **What impact it had** — on the team, the project, the person on the receiving end.
- **What you'd like instead** — specific, behavioral.
- **An invitation to respond** — feedback is a conversation, not a verdict.

## Continuous feedback planning

If the user wants to build the habit, walk them through Fournier's three steps:

1. **Know your people.** For each direct report, write down: their current level, their goals (if any), their strengths, their development areas. Use prior reviews if you have them. If you don't know any of these for someone, that's your next 1-1 topic.

2. **Observe them.** Set a weekly goal: identify one specific thing each report did that deserves praise. Not "good job on the project" — something specific. This forces actual attention and catches the failure mode of obsessing over problems while ignoring strengths.

3. **Provide lightweight, regular feedback.** Mostly positive, specific, in the moment. Save corrective feedback for when there's a real pattern or a real example. Coaching is a bonus on top, not the baseline.

## Review setup: cadence and timing

- **Frequency**: every 6 months. Annual is too long; quarterly is too short.
- **Anchors**: typically end-of-year and mid-year (e.g., December and June).
- **Lead time**: start preparation ~3 weeks before the meeting.
- **If company only requires annual reviews**, do mid-year reviews anyway. You're running a team well, not checking a compliance box.

Reasoning for 6 months: long enough to have done substantive work to reflect on; short enough to course-correct.

## Booking the meetings

Use the existing 1-1 slot — just rename it for that week. The hour is already there. Always private room. Face-to-face if at all possible (video call if remote). Never breakout area, never at desks.

## Peer feedback

For each report, select 3–5 peers to give feedback. Mix angles:

- Teammates within their team
- People they collaborate with on other teams
- People they mentor on other teams
- Cross-functional partners (PMs, designers, ops, sales, customer success)
- People at different seniorities

**Don't just pick their best friends.** The point is a multi-angle picture.

### Email template

```
Hello,

If you're receiving this email then it's because I would love some peer
feedback on [Name] (subject of this email). You've been picked because
you either work closely with this person or have been managed by them.
Ideally I'd like this feedback within the next two weeks.

Peer feedback is one of the most insightful parts of someone's review.
The content between a manager and report shouldn't be that surprising,
but seeing how they show up to colleagues outside that relationship
brings a fuller picture.

Please try to answer the following:

  • What has this person excelled at in the last six months?
  • What should this person do differently and why?
  • What other feedback would you like to give?
  • Would you like to give this feedback anonymously?

Just replying to this email is fine.

Thanks!
```

Color the tracker cell yellow when sent. Green when received.

- If feedback is one-liners, follow up and ask them to expand.
- If a piece of feedback is overly harsh, defamatory, or framed badly, you can edit before passing on. Rare but acceptable.
- If you don't hear back after a week, send **one** polite nudge. After that, find someone else if you can. Don't be annoying — people are busy.

## Pre-review surprise check

Before review season opens, work through this checklist for each direct report:

1. What rating / level are they likely to get?
2. Have they been told things consistent with that rating throughout the year?
3. Is there any corrective feedback that will appear in the review they haven't heard from you in a 1-1?
4. Is there any praise that will appear in the review you haven't already given them?

Anything in row 3 or 4 needs a 1-1 conversation **before** the review is delivered. Not the day before — actual prior conversation.

## The written review form

Two parts they fill in (before the meeting); two parts you fill in (before the meeting). They read yours; you read theirs. Both come to the meeting ready to discuss. The form is a shared doc — visible writing builds trust; both sides can leave comments as you write.

### Their sections

**Achievements**
> Think about your achievements, big or small, over the last six months. What have you accomplished that makes you proud and why?

**Reflection**
> How do you feel in your role right now? Has that changed for the better or worse since your last review?

**Development**
> Is there anything that could have gone better during the period? What skills do you think you need to develop?

**The future**
> Consider your future at the company. Where are you aiming to go? What would you ideally like to accomplish or work on? Or are you happy where you are now?

**Support**
> What sort of support do you need to get where you want to go?

### Your sections

**Achievements**
> What has this person done in the last six months that has impressed you? What do you consider their biggest achievements?

Resist just repeating what they wrote. Bring your unique vantage point — what *you* saw across the team that they may not see themselves.

**Development**
> Where do you think this person should focus on developing? What traits and skills could they work on, and how can you help them get there?

Note: not titled "failures." Even great staff need to be pushed further. This is hardest for top performers.

**Peer feedback summary**
> What were the key themes from this person's peer feedback? Does it reinforce what you both already know, or are there any new observations?

Include the verbatim feedback (anonymized if appropriate) in a separate linked doc.

### Writing time

~1 hour per review, longer for content-heavy ones. Block real time. Reviews packed with both good and bad content always take longer. Get them written with plenty of time before the meeting — both sides need time to read and absorb.

## Bias traps (check before sending)

> From Fournier — before the review goes to anyone, scan for:

- **Recency bias** — am I overweighting the last 2 months because that's what I remember? Check 1-1 notes from earlier in the period.
- **Halo / horns** — one big thing they did well (or badly) is coloring everything else. Make sure each section can stand on its own examples.
- **Scattershot peer feedback** — one reviewer says "sloppy work" and no one else does. Is the work sloppy, or does that reviewer have higher standards than the team? Use judgment; don't pass every grudge through.
- **The "potential" trap** — "He has potential" without performance to match is often bias. Real potential shows up as going the extra mile, insightful suggestions, helping in neglected areas — not as credentials or charm.

## The 5 stages framing for hard reviews

> From Stanier — when a review contains hard news, people typically move through:

1. Ignore it
2. Deny it
3. Blame others
4. Assume responsibility
5. Find a solution

Sharing the written form in advance lets them move through 1–4 privately. The meeting can then focus on 5. This is the entire reason for the written-first format.

## The meeting

If preparation was done right, the meeting is easy. The temptation is to walk through the form linearly — don't. Split the hour into roughly:

- **1/3 reflective discussion** — about the period that passed
- **1/3 forward-thinking discussion** — about the future
- **1/3 collaborative goal-drafting** — for the next 6 months

Use leading questions for the first two thirds:

- "What about that achievement made you feel so proud? Do you feel like that often?"
- "You mentioned wanting to develop X. How do you think you can do that, and how can I support?"
- "How do you feel about the team? Who do you most like working with? Anyone you find harder?"
- "How challenging is the work compared to a year ago?"
- "How does your current role compare to where you expected to be?"
- "What do you think of *my* performance? What can I do to support you better?"

Keep the thought bubble over their head. You wrote the document; this meeting is them making sense of it.

For the goals discussion, don't finalize — draft. Capture initial ideas. Continue in 1-1s afterward.

### Delivery hygiene

- Hand them the document (or share the doc) the evening before, so they can read it once before the meeting. Prevents the conversation being them reading silently while you sit there.
- Walk through each section. Don't let them skip strengths to obsess over gaps. Many will try.
- If there's a numeric rating less than top, come prepared with concrete examples of what a higher rating would look like.
- **No surprises.** If anything in this review will be new news, you have a delivery problem, not a content problem — and you need to flag it as such. Should this have come up months ago?

## Pay — decouple it

**The big rule:** don't deliver pay news inside the performance review meeting.

Why: when people know pay is coming at the end, they don't engage with the performance discussion. They sit there waiting for the number. Then they're either thinking about what to do with the bonus or seething it wasn't enough. Either way, the performance conversation is lost.

Better approaches:

- Tell pay news in a separate meeting (or email if they prefer) at a different time.
- Ask them in advance how they'd like to be told — some prefer in person, some prefer email. Find out and respect it.
- Come prepared with the reasoning: how percentages were calculated, performance vs. company performance, whether they're a special case.
- Leave the door open for pushback. Even if you can't change it this year, frame it as a catalyst for future conversations about what they'd need to do to reach their target compensation.

## On firing and PIPs (performance improvement plans)

If review writing reveals that someone is genuinely below expectations:

- Most companies require a PIP before firing. Follow your HR process.
- **The rule of no surprises applies hardest here.** If a PIP is the first time someone hears they're underperforming, you (or their previous manager) failed.
- Keep written records of negative feedback. Even without HR — email is enough.
- Fournier's warning: *don't put anyone on a PIP whom you wouldn't be happy to lose.* Smart employees treat a surprise PIP as the signal to leave, and the goodwill goes with them.

This crosses into hard-conversation territory — if the user is in this zone, also consult the hard-conversations skill.

## Forcing functions

- **Refuse vague feedback.** "Communication could be better" is not feedback. Push for the specific incident, the specific impact, the specific behavior to change.
- **Refuse to bury bad news.** If the user is hedging, name it: "It sounds like you're trying to soften 'this person isn't ready for promotion' — they deserve to hear that clearly with the reasoning." Direct is kinder.
- **Refuse to skip strengths.** If a draft is 20% strengths and 80% gaps — even for an underperformer — flag it. That's an attack, not a review. Rebalance.
- **Surface implied promotions.** If the review is "they're crushing it at everything," ask: is it time to promote? If not, why not? People unambiguously exceeding their level either get promoted or eventually leave.
- **If the user is doing reviews once a year only**, push for adding a mid-year. The investment pays for itself.
- **If user is going into a review with content the report hasn't heard before**, stop. Have those conversations first, in 1-1s. The review is for synthesis, not delivery.
- **If user picked 3 peers from the same team**, push to diversify the angles.
- **If user wants to talk pay in the review**, push back hard. It destroys the conversation.
- **If user is filling in the form an hour before the meeting**, this is going badly. Reschedule, or skip and do it properly next cycle.
- **If user has nothing in the "future" section**, they haven't thought about where this person could go. Fix that before the meeting.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't write reviews from thin context. If the user gives you two sentences, push back and ask for examples, 1-1 notes, peer feedback.
- Don't generate corporate HR-template language. The tone is direct and human; match that.
- Don't suggest skipping peer feedback to save time. It's the most valuable input.
- Don't help the user soften feedback into vagueness. Specifics, in writing, with care.
- Don't suggest the user "have a difficult conversation" without helping them prepare for it. That's a copout.
