# Hiring and Interviewing

Sources:
- James Stanier, *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, ch. 7 ("Join Us!")
- Camille Fournier, *The Manager's Path*, ch. 7 and 9

Two organizing principles:
1. **Pause before opening any req.** Most managers default to "more senior engineers" without thinking carefully about *who* the team actually needs.
2. **Every candidate gets a good experience.** Even the ones you reject. They might reapply, refer someone, or end up on your team next year. Be professional and prompt.

The two failure modes the books push back against most: hiring out of desperation, and using "culture fit" as a vibe check that filters out everyone unlike the existing team.

## When this skill is loaded — identify mode

- **Who to hire** — deciding role, level, skills before opening any req.
- **JD writing** — drafting or critiquing a job description.
- **Loop / funnel design** — structuring the stages and what each owns.
- **Pre-interview prep** — interview tomorrow with X for role Y; what to focus on.
- **Resume review** — quick filter; reject promptly.
- **Screening / first / final interview** — running a specific stage.
- **Take-home tests** — should I use one; how to scope it.
- **Debrief and decision** — interviews done, hire or not.
- **Reference checks** — who to call, what to ask.
- **The offer** — pay decision, negotiation.
- **Rejection** — kind, prompt rejection letter.
- **Calibration concerns** — my loop keeps producing bad hires / passing on good people.

## Who to hire — the pre-req question

Three traps to call out before any req is opened:

1. **"Just hire more engineers."** Whatever the bottleneck, the reflex is more engineers. But maybe the team needs more QA, a PM, a designer, an SRE, or an agile coach.
2. **"Hire the most senior person possible."** Iron triangle: senior + fast = expensive; fast + cheap = junior; senior + cheap = slow. You can't have all three.
3. **"Hire someone like us / culture fit."** People *add* to culture, they don't fit into it. See *Culture fit, done right* below.

Before opening any req, ask:

- Where does the team consistently bottleneck? Who would unblock it?
- Where is the team thinnest on seniority — and could that level be brought in?
- What does *the team* think you need? Ask them.

### The "too much talent" effect

> From Stanier — in highly interdependent teams (soccer, basketball, software), talent improves performance up to a point and then *hurts* it. A team of all stars stops coordinating; a team with a couple of stars and supporting players plays better.

Applied: don't build a team of all senior engineers. Mix senior + mid + junior. Seniors get the satisfaction of mentoring; juniors grow; the team coordinates.

### Traits to actually hire for

Not these:
- Background or education similar to yours
- Sense-of-humor compatibility
- Someone you'd drink with
- Same hobbies

These instead:
- Motivation
- Willingness to learn
- Collaboration skills
- Good communication
- Empathy and kindness
- Diverse background and viewpoint
- Alignment with company values and ethics

### On hiring friends

It's fine — *if* they'd be good. But:

- You're biased; remove yourself from the formal evaluation. Greet them, catch up at the end, that's it.
- Talk explicitly about the friendship-becoming-management dynamic before they accept. If you can't do that comfortably, recommend them to a different team.
- Friends can't be treated differently once hired. That's favoritism.

## JD writing

A good JD answers four questions (Fournier):

1. **What does this person do day-to-day?** Concrete. Not "drive innovation" — "lead a team of 4–6 engineers building X."
2. **What level / scope is the role?** Map to your career ladder if you have one. For senior roles, distinguish **management track** vs. **IC track** if both exist. Fournier specifically argues for parallel ladders.
3. **What must they have already done?** Distinguish must-haves from nice-to-haves rigorously.
4. **What's the team / company context?** Culture, scale, stage, what the engineering culture is like.

### Structure (Stanier's five sections)

#### 1. Describe the company

Nontechnical, mission-led, two or three sentences. Aim for the reader to think "I'd love to contribute to that."

```
At [Company], we make the world's most popular [thing]. We're on a
mission to [outcome]. We are used by [scale of users], and we [tangible
impact]. We make [target users' lives or jobs better in a specific way].
```

#### 2. Describe the role

Not a bullet list of generic engineer tasks. Sell the opportunity:

- Why is this role important to the whole company?
- What does it actually entail, with concrete examples?
- What's the team like, the working style, the location flexibility?

Use concrete example projects so candidates can picture day one.

#### 3. Describe who you're looking for

Not a laundry list. Broad traits and example experiences. Warm and inviting, not gatekeeping.

```
We're looking for individuals that are curious about the possibility of
technology, are eager to learn, and are diligent and kind. We trust you
to do well, and you will do so together with us.

You'll probably have around X years of industry experience, but we care
less about years and more about what you've been doing.

We believe in radical candor and healthy debate, strong opinions loosely
held. Great ideas can come from anybody, no matter where they are in
the company.
```

#### 4. Salary and benefits

**Publish the salary range.** If the company resists, ask why. Hidden ranges typically mean the company knows it underpays.

List meaningful benefits: healthcare, retirement, real perks. Skip the ping-pong table, snacks, and beer fridge — that's the noise of the 2010s, not the signal of the 2020s.

#### 5. How to apply

Concrete instructions. Email address, resume, optional short cover letter. Mention if part-time is welcome. Mention diversity if you mean it — and back it up with practice.

### Style and gender-neutrality

Three rules:

1. **Don't make a long list of "required" skills.** A 2014 HP study found men apply at ~60% match; women apply only at ~100% match. Long lists filter out qualified women.
2. **Don't use gender-coded language.** Words like "competitive," "dominant," "rockstar," "ninja" code masculine. Words like "support," "collaborate" code feminine. Use the [gender-decoder tool](http://gender-decoder.katmatfield.com) to check.
3. **Focus on work, not hours-in-seat.** Mention flexible / part-time if available. Describe outcomes, not face time.

## Loop design

The principle that prevents most loop problems: **each interview owns specific signals.** If you can't say what signal an interview is meant to produce, the loop is broken. Don't try to assess everything in every slot.

For engineers, signals to distribute across the loop:
- **Technical depth** — can they code / design at the level expected
- **Technical breadth** — do they have the experience the role requires
- **Collaboration** — do they work well in a team setting
- **Culture / values fit** — do they share the team's working values

### Engineer funnel (default)

Six stages — filter hard early; commit deeper later:

1. **Resume review** — quick filter.
2. **Screening call** — 15–30 min, recruiter or hiring manager.
3. **First interview** — hiring manager + one team peer, 1 hour. Includes a collaborative technical exercise.
4. **Optional take-home test** — only when needed (see *Take-home tests* below).
5. **Final interview** — 2–3 more team peers, plus possible exec / cross-functional drop-in. For anyone above mid-level, include a system-design slot.
6. **Offer** — or rejection.

### Manager loop

> From Fournier — hiring managers needs a different shape. Conversation alone doesn't surface management instinct.

1. **Hiring manager screen** — basic fit, role context.
2. **1-1 role-play** — have the people who'd report to this manager interview them. Ask them to help with a real problem one of those engineers has right now. Or to role-play a 1-1 with someone underperforming. You learn more about a manager's instincts in 30 minutes of role-play than in an hour of "tell me about your management philosophy."
3. **Team debugging** — ask about a project that ran late and what they did. Or role-play addressing an engineer who's thinking about quitting.
4. **Management philosophy** — what do they think the job is? How do they delegate? How do they stay hands-on? An experienced manager with no philosophy is a red flag.
5. **Technical screen** — calibrated to the role. Don't make a non-coding director do a coding screen, but do verify they can establish credibility with engineers through design / architecture questions on systems they've built.
6. **Presentation** — for senior roles. How do they handle a room, structure thoughts, answer questions? Don't overweight this — plenty of excellent managers aren't strong public speakers.
7. **Cross-functional peer interview** — product, design, or another tech lead they'd partner with.
8. **Reference checks** — non-negotiable for management hires (see *Reference checks* below).

## Pre-interview prep

When the user has an interview coming up:

1. **What signal is this interview supposed to produce?** If you can't say, the loop is broken. Each interview owns specific signals; don't try to assess everything.
2. **Review their resume and prior interviews** if any. Spot the things you want to probe.
3. **Prepare 2–3 anchor questions** for the signals you own, plus follow-ups for likely answers.
4. **Plan the time**: intro (3–5 min), main content (40 min), their questions (10 min), close (2 min). Don't run over and steal time from their questions — what they ask tells you a lot.

For values / culture-fit signals specifically: don't ask "are you a culture fit?" Ask behavioral questions that surface the values you care about:

- **Collaboration**: "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a teammate on technical direction. How did it resolve?"
- **Ownership**: "Tell me about something that broke in production that you owned end-to-end."
- **Mentorship**: "What's been your experience helping more junior engineers grow?"

## Resume review

Look for:

- Right level of experience for the role
- Used your tech stack or similar enough
- Time and care in the application
- Within commutable distance, remote-friendly fit, or willing to relocate

Don't over-read:

- **Resume gaps** aren't necessarily red flags (parenting, travel, illness, a band).
- **Lots of job changes** aren't necessarily red flags (startups that didn't take off).
- **Bias check**: if you're drawn to a resume, ask why. Pair-review with someone if uncertain.

Reject promptly with a kind, brief letter (template under *Rejection* below).

## Screening call

15–30 min video call. Cover:

- Introduce yourself; tell them how long the call will take.
- Tell them about the company and the role.
- Admin: relocation, visa, notice period, start timeframe.
- Their current role and responsibilities.
- Why this role interested them specifically.
- Salary expectations.
- Their questions.
- Next steps.

No brain-teasers. No tricks. This is a sense-check, not an interrogation.

## First interview

1 hour. Hiring manager + 1 future teammate. Tell candidate in advance:
- Duration
- Who they'll meet
- Format: conversation + collaborative technical exercise + their questions
- Dress code (so they don't show up in a suit at a hoodie company)

### How to be a great interviewer

- **Prepare** — read their application, have it in front of you.
- **Make them comfortable** — greet at reception, walk them to the room, offer a drink, ask about their day.
- **Be yourself** — they're picking you too. Don't put on a manager persona.
- **Describe what's coming before it happens** — "We're going to do a whiteboard exercise now. There's no right answer; we'll work through it together." Reduces anxiety.
- **Allow for nerves** — younger or less practiced candidates will fluster. Be warm; help where needed.
- **Detect silence and intervene** — if they're stuck, ask if they want help or to switch.
- **Invite questions and conversation** — make it two-way.
- **Make notes** — you won't remember after a week of interviews.
- **Be aware of your unconscious biases** — record what they *said*, not your interpretation. Sleep on decisions when possible.

### Question structure for first interview

Introduction: company and team.

Their current job:
- What have you been working on?
- Team / department / company size?
- What's a typical day?
- How does software get defined, coded, and shipped?
- If still employed: what's making you want to leave?

Technical knowledge:
- How is your current codebase or architecture structured? What's good and bad?
- How does testing work? Whose responsibility is it?
- Favorite framework / language / library and why?
- Any experience with [our stack]?

General technology:
- What IDE do you use? What do you like about it?
- Most exciting piece of software you've used recently?
- When did you get your first computer?

Wrapping up:
- Their questions
- Next steps in the process

### Technical exercises — do's and don'ts

**Do:**
- Work through a problem together. Don't sit in silence.
- Pick problems with multiple correct approaches; the interesting part is optimization.
- Draw from real problems your team has actually faced (anonymized).
- Pair-coding format is more humane than whiteboard.
- Respect their nervousness.

**Don't:**
- Create an examination atmosphere.
- Use brain teasers ("estimate the streetlights in the UK").
- Assume everyone is a math whiz.
- Insist code compiles. Pseudocode is fine.

A great alternative to code: a system-design exercise. Stick figures with phones. "Let's design a chat application together. Where would we start? What language? Where's the conversation history stored?" Follow the candidate's strengths.

## Take-home tests

Optional. Don't use them if a candidate already has strong public signals (impressive GitHub, well-known previous employers). For nervous candidates and recent grads, take-homes are kinder than whiteboarding.

Rules:

- **Scope is crystal clear.** Provide a starter repo or skeleton. Don't make them set up an environment.
- **Time-bounded to ~2 hours.** Anything longer disrespects their time and effectively makes them work for free.
- **Mirrors the actual work.** Bug fix in a sample of your codebase, an API integration, etc. Not abstract puzzles.

## Final interview

Candidate has met you + 1 in first interview. Final adds 2–3 more peers, plus optionally an exec or CTO drop-in. Tell the candidate about any exec — surprise C-level appearances are intimidating.

Format option: start with everyone for intros, then leave so the candidate can talk to future peers without the hiring manager in the room. Come back at the end. Two-way: the candidate is choosing you too.

Don't sit them across a panel like an interrogation. Seating matters.

## Debrief and decision

### Common failure modes

- **Letting the loudest opinion anchor the decision.** Collect written feedback before anyone discusses, ideally with a hire/no-hire vote already submitted.
- **"Culture fit" used as a vibe check.** Always ask: *which values* did this person clash with or align with? "I just didn't click with them" is bias, not signal.
- **Falling for charm in management hires.** Managers can interview well — they're good at communication — and still be ineffective on the job. Probe the role-play results more than the polished answers.
- **Hiring against gap rather than for strength.** If you're hiring because the team is drowning, you'll be tempted to take whoever can start Monday. Resist.

### Decision framework

For each interviewer, get:

- **Hire / no-hire** (binary — push past "weak hire / weak no-hire" hedging).
- **What signal did they get?** (positive and negative)
- **What's the strongest concern?**

Then synthesize. Strong opposition from one interviewer with specific reasons usually beats lukewarm support from three. But watch for the lone dissenter who can't articulate why — sometimes that's bias.

**The rule when on the fence:** don't hire. *Empty seat > bad hire.* Don't lower the bar because the team is drowning. Drowning teams that hire too fast keep drowning.

## Reference checks

Fournier's hard rule: **always do reference checks for management hires.** Skipping is a disservice to your team. Even for engineers, reference checks catch what interviews miss. The candidate picks the references — that's fine; you can still learn a lot.

Good questions:

- "What was your working relationship?"
- "What does this person do best?"
- "Where do they struggle? What do they need to work on?"
- "What kind of manager / teammate gets the best out of them?"
- "Would you work with them again? Would you hire them?"
- For management references: "How did their team feel about working for them?"

Listen for hesitations, evasions, faint praise. "They were... very smart" is not a recommendation.

## The offer

Pay decisions consider three things:

- Salaries of their peers at your company (fairness)
- Market rate in your area (competitiveness)
- Your budget

**Don't lowball.** Don't expect to play a negotiation game. Offer a number you can defend with reasoning, and tell them the reasoning.

If the offered salary is at the top of the band, say it's because you see them as senior end of the role. If lower, say it's because of [specific gap], and outline what unlocks future raises.

Be prepared for them to decline. It happens. Let it go.

## Rejection

Reject promptly, kindly, briefly. Template:

```
Dear [Name],

Thank you for your application to the [role]. We're sorry to inform you
that you haven't progressed to the next stage of the interview process.
We loved your application, but we are looking for people who [specific
reason — more experience in X, deeper Y, etc.].

We're growing, so keep checking our website for other roles. We'd love
for you to apply again in the future.
```

## Culture fit, done right

The framing: culture fit means shared *values*, not shared friendships. The "would you be stuck in an airport with this person" test is a friendship test, and friendship tests correlate with school / class / race / gender — they discriminate without telling you so. Stanier's version of the same point: "culture isn't a fixed thing — people *add* to culture, they don't fit into it."

To do culture fit well:

1. **Write down your values explicitly.** "Engineering excellence." "Collaboration over heroics." "Direct, kind communication." Whatever yours are. If you can't name them, you can't hire for them.
2. **For each value, define what it looks like behaviorally.** What does someone exhibiting this value *do* in a meeting? In a code review? Under pressure?
3. **Design interview probes** for those behaviors (see *Pre-interview prep* for examples).
4. **Debrief on values explicitly** — not "did they fit" but "which value did they exemplify or clash with."

Done this way, culture fit *expands* the candidate pool by surfacing alignment in people who don't look or sound like the existing team. Done as a vibe check, it shrinks the pool to clones of the founders.

## Calibration concerns

If the user says their loop keeps producing bad hires:

- **Are loop members aligned on the bar?** Calibration session: review notes from recent hires (good and bad), agree on what signals would have caught the bad ones.
- **Is the loop consistent?** Same loop for every candidate, same questions, same scoring rubric.
- **Are interviewers trained?** Many engineers interview by gut. Brief them on what signals they own and how to elicit them.
- **Are you over-weighting any single signal?** "Crushed the coding round" doesn't mean hire if the collaboration round was a disaster.

If the user says their loop keeps passing on people the team later wishes they'd hired:

- **Is the bar miscalibrated?** Some teams set the bar so high they only hire people who would have been overqualified.
- **Is the loop biased against certain backgrounds?** Trial questions on people who've shipped real software but didn't go to a "top" school, or who came from non-traditional paths.
- **Are you optimizing for a profile that's not what the role needs?** A staff engineer doing system design interviews on someone you're hiring to ship features fast might be filtering for the wrong things.

## Forcing functions

- **If user is hiring "more engineers" reactively**, pause and ask what the actual bottleneck is.
- **If "we just need someone" appears**, check the bar. Desperation drives bad hires.
- **If JD has a long list of required skills**, push to compress and convert most to "nice to have."
- **If JD uses words like "rockstar" or "ninja"**, flag the gender-coding research.
- **If JD doesn't publish salary**, ask if the company can be moved on this. It's a real signal to candidates.
- **If "culture fit" appears in feedback without specifics**, demand specifics. Which value?
- **If the loop has no role-play for a management hire**, flag it. Conversation alone doesn't surface management instinct.
- **If reference checks aren't planned for a management hire**, push back hard. This is the cheapest signal you can get.
- **If user is conferring with co-interviewers before each writes independent feedback**, that's bias amplification. Independent first, then discuss.
- **If user is about to hire someone with significant concerns**, name them. "You've described X, Y, Z concerns. Do you want to hire this person, or do you want to keep looking?" Don't rubber-stamp.
- **If user describes a brain-teaser exercise**, redirect to a realistic technical problem.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't write JDs from a one-line prompt. Push for context.
- Don't generate interview questions that test trivia (favorite color of the bikeshed). Tests should reflect real work.
- Don't help the user rationalize a hire they have concerns about. Surface the concerns; let them decide.
- Don't recommend hiring fast at the expense of hiring well. An empty seat is cheaper than a bad fill.
- Don't recommend rejecting candidates without reading their application properly.
