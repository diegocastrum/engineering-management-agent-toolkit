# Information and Politics

Source: James Stanier, *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, chapter 12 ("The Information Stock Exchange").

Information is a manager's currency. Stanier's argument: act like a gatekeeper, not a spy. Share *just enough*, consistently, with everyone. And recognize that workplace politics aren't optional — but they can be navigated positively.

## When this skill is loaded

Identify mode:

- **Information triage** — user has info and isn't sure if/how to share
- **Sharing strategy** — how to communicate something sensitive
- **Politics navigation** — building consensus, handling factions
- **Conflict navigation** — disagreement that won't resolve cleanly
- **Boundary check** — the user is being told something they shouldn't know

## Spies vs. gatekeepers

Two postures toward information.

**A spy:**
- Obtains information without permission of the holder
- Obtains information for another party's benefit
- Uses deceitful means

**A gatekeeper:**
- Decides what information should be heard by which parties
- Decides when that information should be shared
- Decides how that information should be framed

**You are a gatekeeper.** Be careful you don't become a spy by accident — when someone slips you a spreadsheet you weren't meant to see, when someone wants to extract info from you at the bar, when a junior offers you gossip in exchange for your trust.

If you receive information you shouldn't have, **tell the sender, don't open it, delete or return.** That's the gatekeeper's discipline.

## Two governing principles

Above any specific rule, two ethics:

1. **Hippocratic Oath:** *first, do no harm.*
2. **Golden Rule:** *treat others as you would like to be treated.*

When in doubt, both apply.

## The three categories of information

Categorize every piece of sensitive information into one of three buckets:

| Category | Examples | How to handle |
|---|---|---|
| **Completely confidential** | Upcoming layoffs (specific names), individual PIPs, in-progress investigations | Don't share. Don't hint. Don't confirm rumors. |
| **Closed box** | Pay review in progress (no individual numbers yet), ongoing reorg planning, hiring decisions in flight | Acknowledge the *process* exists. Don't disclose contents. |
| **Open box** | Team membership, project status, public roadmap, who's working on what | Share freely. |

**Common mistake:** treating closed-box information as completely confidential. Silence creates anxiety. In the absence of information, people fill in the worst. Tell people the pay review is in progress; you just won't share individual numbers until it's done. That's the gatekeeper's craft.

## Consistency in sharing

Two rules across all categories:

1. **Be consistent across people.** Don't tell some what you don't tell others. Don't share to build rapport. Don't share because someone's a friend.

2. **Share just enough.** Not nothing; not everything. Enough to be informative; not so much that confidentiality breaks.

### Test: should I tell this person?

Only share with someone if:

- It is relevant to them and their job
- They have a reason to know
- It benefits you both (unlocks further conversation, enables better decisions)
- You're only sharing what they need to know
- They can be trusted to keep sensitive parts sensitive

Otherwise, ask yourself why you're sharing. Often the urge is to feel close to them, to build rapport, to seem in-the-know. Those are bad reasons.

## Delivering bad news

When the information is hard — layoffs, project cancellations, a sudden change — follow this sequence:

### 1. Listen and observe (in the room where the news lands)

Separate facts from emotion. Hear the senior leader out. Take notes. Identify the underlying facts.

### 2. Digest before delivering

Create space between receiving and re-delivering. Sleep on it if possible. Ask yourself:

- What does this actually mean for the team, with emotion stripped out?
- Does anything actually change for them?
- Who will react strongest? Why?

### 3. Communicate to your team

Reframe the message. Don't lie; do strip the emotional charge and add framing the team needs. "Our project is being deprioritized" is not "the CEO is furious about how we performed."

Choose the right medium per person:
- Sensitive personal news → in-person, 1-1
- Team change → in-person team conversation, then written follow-up
- Org-wide change → cascade through individual 1-1s first, then team meeting, then written

**Never broadcast hard news to a wide group as the first delivery.** Always cascade from highest-impact individuals outward.

### 4. Lean on peers and your own manager

You're processing too. Talk to a peer. Talk to your manager. Don't carry this alone — but do contain it to appropriate confidants, not the team.

## Leave nobody behind

You have a duty to keep people informed. Strategies:

- **Weekly digest email** — what you're working on (separately from tickets), interesting things from your management work, upcoming decisions
- **Mention non-ticket work in standup** — "I'm writing reviews this week" / "I'm planning hires for next year"
- **Fact-finding missions** — talk to your network, gather info, write it up for the team

When in doubt, share open-box information liberally. The asymmetry is real: under-sharing creates anxiety; over-sharing rarely hurts.

## Workplace politics

"Politics" gets used to mean negative behavior. That's a subset. Real definition: *how people in groups make decisions.* Politics happens. You can engage positively or negatively.

### Where politics arise

Three structural tensions:

1. **The org chart** — debates with your boss, with reports, with reaches up and down
2. **Close-knit informal groups** — friendship cliques, shared-interest groups, alumni
3. **Influential individuals** — tenure, celebrity, bargaining power

You can't avoid politics. You can choose how you engage.

### Positive uses of politics

**Connecting with teams and groups.** Map your org. Who makes decisions? Who's influential? Who are the natural factions and what do they care about? Build relationships *before* you need them.

**Building consensus.** Big decisions in big orgs go through informal discussion first. For a controversial idea, start small: 1-1 conversations with influential and close-to-the-issue people. Get their input. Refine. Then expand the proposal. Don't drop a fully-formed plan as a fait accompli on a wide group.

**Being yourself.** Don't put on personas. Be open, transparent, respectfully critical. Disagree and commit when needed (see below). Have the confidence to be proved wrong. Never push agendas purely for personal gain.

**Building your network.** Already covered as a positive activity. Politics reinforces why — informed peers help you sense-check ideas before they need formal pushing.

### Negative uses of politics (avoid)

**Misuse of power.** Just ordering people because you can erodes respect over time. Picture each manager with an "energy bar" — overrides deplete it; alignment refills it. Use overrides sparingly.

**Level-jumping.** Going over someone's head bypasses them and signals disrespect. Skip-level *meetings* are different (your manager occasionally meeting with your reports) — that's legitimate. Routinely circumventing your manager (or your reports' managers) is not.

**Being unprofessional.** Loaded emotion, ulterior motives, treating people poorly. Trust erodes; future opportunities close. Karma operates in orgs as in life.

**Going rogue.** Building consensus is slow; just doing it solo is fast. But unsanctioned work tends to ignite conflicts you didn't see coming. If you can't build consensus, your idea may be weaker than you think — or you haven't framed it right.

**Personal gain.** Using influence to enrich yourself, lobby in friends for referral bonuses, force others into situations they don't want. Just don't. Ever.

## Disagree and commit

When a group can't reach consensus and a decision must be made, the principle of *disagree and commit*:

- You state clearly you don't agree.
- You commit to executing the decision once made.
- You don't continue to fight it after the boundary is crossed.

This unblocks decisions while letting honest disagreement live. The alternatives are worse: phantom agreement (passive resistance), or paralysis (no decision at all).

When delivering a disagree-and-commit decision down the chain: you can acknowledge "this wasn't my preferred outcome; here's what we're doing and why." You can't undermine the decision to your team while still being its messenger. That's the worst option.

## Output format

For a "should I share this?" question, walk through:

```
1. Category: [completely confidential / closed box / open box]
2. Relevant to whom: [list]
3. Why those specifically: [reasoning]
4. What to share: [exact content]
5. What not to share: [what stays out]
6. Medium: [1-1 / team / written / etc.]
7. Sequencing: [who first, then who, then who]
```

For drafting a sensitive message, produce the actual text with framing notes.

## Forcing functions

- **If the user is about to broadcast hard news to a wide group as first delivery**, redirect to cascade approach.
- **If the user wants to share to build rapport with one person**, ask what makes them the right person — relevance, need to know, benefit. If those don't hold, don't share.
- **If the user is holding closed-box information silently** when they could acknowledge the process exists, push for sharing the existence (not the content).
- **If the user is asked to keep something they shouldn't be keeping** (illegal, unethical, harmful to others) — that's not a politics question, that's a values question. Don't help them keep it.
- **If the user is about to level-jump**, ask why they can't go through normal channels. Skip-level *meetings* yes; circumvention no.
- **If a decision needs disagree-and-commit** and the user keeps lobbying after the decision, name it: the boundary's been crossed, time to commit.
- **If the user has just been told confidential info they didn't seek out**, ask what their gatekeeper move is — do they tell the sender, refuse to read it, return it?

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't generate scripts for politically manipulating colleagues.
- Don't help the user keep information that's genuinely harmful to keep (illegal, abusive, etc.).
- Don't endorse going around process to "get things done faster."
- Don't suggest "saying nothing" when "saying something" would resolve anxiety with little risk.
