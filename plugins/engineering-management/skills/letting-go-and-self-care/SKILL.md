# Letting Go and Self-Care

Source: James Stanier, *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, chapter 13 ("Letting Go of Control").

A manager works through others. That requires letting go. Stanier's argument: when you can't let go, your effectiveness drops and your mental health follows. Three frameworks — Stoic trichotomy of control, L-mode/R-mode brain, and outside-of-work habits — help.

## When this skill is loaded

Identify mode:

- **Stoicism mode** — user is worrying about outcomes they don't control
- **Capacity mode** — user is running at 100%+ and feeling it
- **Notification mode** — user can't stop checking, even out of hours
- **Sleep / body mode** — user has sleep, exercise, or recovery problems affecting work
- **Mindfulness mode** — user wants to learn a simple practice
- **Reactive micromanagement** — user is pulling work back under stress

## The trichotomy of control (Stoicism, adapted)

Epictetus: *some things are up to us and some are not.* William Irvine's refinement adds a middle category. Three buckets:

| Bucket | Examples | How to relate to it |
|---|---|---|
| **Complete control** | Your own desires, goals, effort, attitude | Set goals here. Judge yourself here. |
| **No control** | Weather, other people's emotions, market conditions, past events | Don't let your tranquility depend on these. |
| **Some control** | Whether your team ships on time, whether someone stays, whether a deal closes, whether the launch succeeds | Set *internal* goals here, not external. |

The middle bucket is the trap. As a manager, almost everything you do falls there — you have *some* control but not complete control over team outcomes.

**External goal:** "The launch will succeed." (Not fully in your control. Setting this goal is setting yourself up for misery.)

**Internal goal:** "I will lead this launch as well as I can — preparation, alignment, support, decision-making." (Fully in your control. You can hit this regardless of outcome.)

Examples:

- Launching a new product → don't worry about whether it's a success (external). Worry about whether you and the team did the best possible job (internal).
- Staff thinking of leaving → don't worry about making them not leave (external). Counter-offer, accommodate, support — and accept they may go (internal).
- Project running late → don't worry about whether it's late (often external — depends on unknowns). Pull the levers you can: scope, resources, time (internal).

**Forcing function:** when you catch yourself catastrophizing or restless about something out of your control, reframe to the internal goal. What can you actually *do*? Do that. Then let the outcome unfold.

## L-mode and R-mode

From Andy Hunt's *Pragmatic Thinking and Learning*. Your brain has two modes that can't run simultaneously:

- **L-mode (linear)** — sequential, instruction-execution mode. Answering email, working a ticket, replying to messages, processing the agenda.
- **R-mode (rich)** — pattern-matching, holistic, asynchronous search. Finds connections, generates insight. You don't control when it runs.

Both are essential. L-mode executes; R-mode innovates. Modern managerial life is heavily L-mode by default — emails, notifications, meetings, message queues. Without intentional space, R-mode never gets a turn.

### Carve out R-mode time

Block roughly 10% of your week for R-mode — a couple of hours, multiple times, on the calendar. During this time:

- No meetings
- No email
- Away from desk if possible (walk, sofa, café, garden)
- Bring your capture tool (notebook, phone) — when insight emerges, jot it down
- No agenda

Schedule it for when you tend to have ideas — often morning or end of week. After the block, sit down and process what came up. Flesh out the notes. Move to-dos to your list.

This isn't a luxury. R-mode is where your best decisions come from.

## The 85% capacity rule

A common mistake: scheduling yourself at 100% capacity. Then a fire, a personal crisis from a report, an unexpected interview round, a budgeting cycle — and there's no slack. Things drop.

Operate at **85% capacity** by default. The 15% is your buffer for:

- Emergencies
- Reports needing your time
- Unexpected requests from your manager
- Helping a peer
- Sitting and thinking

When asked to take on more, **say no by default** unless it's clearly worth replacing something. The instinct to say yes to look productive is exactly how you become unproductive.

Your manager will not think you're slacking. They'll see the work you actually deliver — which gets better when you're not maxed.

## The notification compulsion

Email, Slack, DMs, ticket comments — all designed to keep you coming back. Each check is a tiny dopamine hit. Over months, this becomes compulsive: you check at lunch, in bed, at weekends, in the shower, on holiday.

Concrete intervention:

- **Batch sessions only.** Email maybe 3x/day. Slack/chat in mindful chunks, not always-on. Close them in between.
- **No notifications on phone for work apps outside of work hours.** Turn them off. If something is so urgent they need to reach you, they'll call.
- **Physical separation.** Phone in another room at night. Laptop closed and stored at weekend.
- **No bed-screen.** This is also a sleep issue (below). Read a book.

The withdrawal is real for the first few days. After that, the world has not ended and you have your evenings back.

## Sleep is the highest-leverage variable

Stanier cites Matthew Walker (*Why We Sleep*): the biggest predictor of having a good work day is the previous night's sleep. Effects of sleep deprivation:

- Reduced concentration and cognitive performance
- Increased risk of neurological/psychiatric conditions long-term
- Worse emotional regulation
- Reduced creativity (R-mode is sleep-fueled)
- Reduced motor performance and aerobic capacity
- Skill consolidation requires sleep — learning happens in sleep

**You cannot "catch up" on weekends.** Sleep is a one-time offer per night.

### Practical sleep rules

| Lever | Why | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Caffeine** | Blocks adenosine receptors that signal sleep; long half-life | Cut off in the morning or early afternoon |
| **Alcohol** | Fragments sleep into brief wake periods (not consciously remembered); kills REM | Reduce, especially within a few hours of bed |
| **Gadgets in bed** | Blue light delays melatonin by up to 50% | Phones/tablets/laptops out of the bedroom |
| **Bedroom temperature** | Core temp must drop to initiate sleep | ~65°F / 18°C |
| **Exercise timing** | Late exercise raises core temp, blocks sleep onset | Don't exercise within 3 hours of bed |
| **Consistent schedule** | Circadian rhythm benefits from regularity | Same bed time / wake time, every day |

Caveat for parents, caregivers, people with sick kids or pets: do your best. You're sacrificing your sleep for someone else, which is the greater good. The rules still apply when you can apply them.

## Movement

Sitting all day is bad for physical and mental health. Studies cited by Stanier link physical inactivity to a wide range of poor outcomes. Movement boosts mood (endorphins), creativity (the walking effect — Stanford research), and sleep quality.

Concrete options at increasing levels of commitment:

1. **5-minute walk per hour** — get up from the desk, walk a loop. Even at home.
2. **Walking 1-1s** — instead of a meeting room, walk together. Stanier strongly recommends. Sit-down feels better at first; walking-and-talking unlocks different conversations.
3. **Walk or cycle commute** when geography allows.
4. **Structured exercise** — running, gym, classes, sport. Build from small.

Don't go from zero to hero. Start small and let the habit form.

## Mindful breathing exercise

If the user is acutely stressed or wants a simple practice, walk them through this. 3-5 minutes:

1. Sit comfortably or lie down. Close eyes (after reading the steps).
2. Notice your breath. Feel it move in and out of your body.
3. Focus on your belly rising and falling. Stay here for a minute.
4. If thoughts intrude, notice them and return attention to the breath. This is normal.
5. Shift attention to the breath at your nostrils. Notice the cool in, warm out. Stay here for a minute.
6. Open eyes gently.

This isn't meditation theater. It's a tool. Two minutes between meetings; five minutes before a hard conversation; ten minutes when stress is acute.

For longer practice: Jon Kabat-Zinn, *Wherever You Go, There You Are*, is a good starting book.

## Reactive micromanagement under stress

Under deadline pressure, the urge to take back delegated work is strong. **Resist.** Reasons it's tempting:

- You know you can do it fast and well
- You're anxious about the outcome
- You feel powerless and want some action

Reasons not to:

- It signals distrust to the person who was doing it
- It cheats them out of a learning opportunity
- You can't actually do everything yourself — math doesn't work
- It scales the problem rather than fixing the cause

When you feel the pull: name it. "I want to take this back because I'm anxious about the deadline." Then ask: is the right move to take it back, or to give more support? Usually it's support.

## Outside-of-work habits matter

Burnout is now a WHO-recognized occupational phenomenon. Symptoms:

- Energy depletion / exhaustion
- Increased mental distance from work, negativism, cynicism
- Reduced professional efficacy

Mental disorders affect ~13% of the global population. Anxiety affects ~300M people. Depression ~160M. You are not alone if you struggle with these — and you are not weak.

If you suspect mental health issues in yourself: talk to someone. Partner, friends, family, your manager, HR, EAP, a therapist. This is beyond what a self-help book or a manager skill can fix. Get real support.

## Forcing functions

- **If the user is anxious about an outcome they don't fully control**, apply the trichotomy. What's the internal goal?
- **If the user has no R-mode time blocked this week**, schedule some now.
- **If the user is running at 100%+ capacity**, identify what comes off the list.
- **If the user is taking back delegated work**, pause and ask why. The reason is usually anxiety, not necessity.
- **If the user reports they can't sleep** because they're thinking about work, work the sleep rules and the notification rules.
- **If the user is checking email on holiday**, that's the compulsion. Phone in a drawer; if it's truly urgent they'll call.
- **If the user is heading toward burnout** (exhausted, cynical, ineffective), this is not a productivity problem. It's a health one. Suggest professional support.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't moralize about hustle culture beyond what's necessary.
- Don't prescribe specific sleep aids, supplements, or medication.
- Don't act as a therapist. If the user is in distress beyond self-help, point them to real support: their healthcare provider, EAP if available, a therapist, crisis lines.
- Don't suggest "just relax." This skill is mechanical advice, not platitudes.
