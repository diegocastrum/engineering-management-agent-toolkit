# Manager Toolkit: Self-Organization

Source: James Stanier, *Become an Effective Software Engineering Manager*, chapter 2 ("Manage Yourself First").

A manager who can't keep their own house in order can't help anyone else. Stanier's argument: keep almost nothing in your head. Use four simple tools, with strict rules.

## When this skill is loaded

Identify what the user needs:

- **Setup mode** — they want to install the system from scratch.
- **Triage mode** — they're overwhelmed *right now* and need a way out.
- **Audit mode** — they want to evaluate where their time goes.
- **Output mode** — they're wondering "what am I even producing?"

## The four tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Calendar** | Organizes your time only — meetings, busy blocks, focus blocks |
| **To-do list** | Organizes your tasks — single source of truth |
| **Email inbox** | Organizes your incoming messages — primary inbox for all channels |
| **Capture system** | A notebook or phone for when you're away from the other three |

**Core principle:** keep as little in your head as possible.

## Tool 1: Calendar

Only put these in your calendar:
- Meetings with other people
- Activities indicating you are busy (lunch, OOO, doctor's appointment)
- Focus blocks ("no meetings please" entries to prevent interruption)

**Strict rules:**
- The calendar is **not** your to-do list — keep tasks elsewhere.
- Block "no meetings please" chunks for deep work — usually a few hours, multiple times per week.
- Make meetings public by default; mark private only when content is sensitive.
- Set 10-minute reminders before each meeting.
- Open your calendar as the first task each morning. Move things around as needed.
- Batch meetings to give yourself flow time when possible.
- Don't let yourself get booked into 8 hours of back-to-back interviews — that's your own fault.

## Tool 2: To-do list

**The rule that makes it work: if it's not on your to-do list, you're not doing it.**

This forces you to keep it up to date and frees mental space. An email asking you to do something? Put it on the list, archive the email.

What good to-do list software gives you:
- Recurring tasks (daily/weekly reminders to check email, prep 1-1s, etc.)
- Due dates and prioritization
- A "Later" view that hides non-today items
- Subtasks and notes
- Categories for different streams of work

Stanier uses Asana. Anything works as long as it has these features. Paper doesn't sync.

**Daily habit:** open it first thing in the morning, last thing at end of day. Drag-prioritize the day. Move what doesn't get done into tomorrow's Later view.

## Tool 3: Email inbox

Treat email as the primary source for all incoming messages. Route chat, tickets, alerts to email if your tools support it.

**Strict rules:**
- **Archive aggressively.** Read and done → archive. Replied → archive. Need to action it → put action on to-do list, archive email.
- **Never delete.** Archive everything; search later. Five years from now you'll need that thread.
- **Unsubscribe** from anything you don't read.
- **Filter rule for important messages** — agree on a keyword with your staff that routes to top.
- **Batch sessions, don't watch the inbox.** Process email 2-3 times a day in mindful chunks. Close it the rest of the time.
- **Inbox zero is a guide, not a doctrine** — don't trade an hour of evening work for getting to zero.

**Most important rule, worth restating:** email is not your to-do list. Action goes to the to-do list, then email gets archived. This is the #1 cause of forgetting things.

## Tool 4: Capture system

For when you're away from the other three. Phone, notebook, anything you'll always have on you. Transfer to to-do list at next opportunity.

Mind your manners — typing into your phone in a meeting can read as disengaged.

## Categorizing your day (Andy Grove)

When you feel unproductive, retroactively categorize activities. Every managerial action falls into one of four buckets (from Grove's *High Output Management*):

- **Information gathering** — reading email, attending updates, kitchen chats. Feeds your knowledge base.
- **Decision-making** — making calls of any size, from holiday approval to architecture
- **Nudging** — contributing your viewpoint to others' decisions; influencing without deciding
- **Being a role model** — leading by example; the behaviors others copy from you

When the day feels lost, walking back through it through these four lenses usually reveals you did real work. It also helps you spot which bucket you're neglecting — typically nudging or role-modeling.

## A manager's output (the equation)

> **Output = output of your team + output of others you influence**

Three corollaries:

1. **You're accountable for the team's output.** If they're not performing, that's on you.
2. **The team's output > your personal output.** This means delegate, coach, mentor — don't do tasks yourself.
3. **The "others you influence" part is real.** Nudging and being a role model contribute to your output through the whole org.

Use the equation as a forcing function when deciding how to spend time: "Does this activity grow my team's output or others' output? If not, why am I doing it?"

## Setup mode

If the user is installing the system fresh, walk them through this order:

1. Pick to-do list software. Asana is fine. So is anything with recurring tasks + Later view.
2. Add daily recurring tasks: "open calendar and review day", "process email", "process email", "prep 1-1 [if scheduled today]".
3. Audit calendar: remove anything that isn't a meeting, busy block, or focus block. Add focus blocks.
4. Archive every email that's been read. Send what's left into the system (action to to-do list, archive).
5. Pick capture tool (phone notes app is fine).
6. Commit to the rule: if it's not on the to-do list, it doesn't happen.

## Triage mode

When the user is currently overwhelmed:

1. Stop. Don't try to "catch up" — that path leads to more overwhelm.
2. Block 30-45 minutes on the calendar right now to triage.
3. Process inbox: action → list, archive; reply → archive; read → archive. Don't reply to low-priority now.
4. Look at the next 48 hours of calendar. Decline what doesn't matter.
5. Pick the 3 most important things for today. The rest goes to Later.
6. Then close email and chat for 90 minutes.

## Audit mode

When the user wants to know where their time goes:

1. For 3-5 days, capture every activity in 30-minute blocks (calendar review at end of day works).
2. Tag each block with one of Grove's four categories.
3. Look at the proportions. What's missing? What's bloated?
4. Common findings: too much information gathering, not enough decision-making or role-modeling.

## Forcing functions

- **If the user uses email as a to-do list**, name it. That's the single highest-leverage fix.
- **If their calendar is also their to-do list**, fix the calendar first — it's poisoning their planning.
- **If they have no focus blocks**, push back. Days made of meetings produce no decisions of consequence.
- **If they say "I just need to work harder"**, no — they need to work differently. Working harder doesn't survive context-switching.
- **If their to-do list is in 3 places**, consolidate. Multiple lists = no list.
- **If they're proud of a 10,000-email inbox**, this is the conversation where you tell them archive isn't delete.

## What this skill should NOT do

- Don't recommend specific software unless asked (Asana is fine as a default, but the tool isn't the point).
- Don't generate productivity systems with more than the four tools. Adding tools is the disease, not the cure.
- Don't moralize about hustle or work hours. The system works at 35 hrs/week or 60.
